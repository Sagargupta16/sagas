# Write an essay

Essays here are chapters in a collection book on the `essays` shelf. The method is Graham-first: essays discover, they don't just assert.

## Preparation

Read `references/essay-craft.md` from the `writing-craft` library; add `references/revision.md` for the editing pass.

## Steps

1. **Find the question.** Turn his topic into a genuine question he doesn't fully have the answer to yet (Graham: start from a question, not a thesis; an essay that only defends a position he already held is a speech, not an essay).
2. **Motive check.** Which of Orwell's four motives drives this piece (sheer egoism, aesthetic enthusiasm, historical impulse, political purpose)? Name it -- it sets the register.
3. **Draft to discover.** Let him talk/draft the meander; capture it. Don't structure prematurely. Note the moments of surprise -- those are the essay (Graham: aim for maximum surprise).
4. **Find the spine.** After the meander: what's the one useful claim? Test it against Graham's usefulness formula -- importance x novelty x correctness x strength. Steelman the strongest counter-position in the essay itself; an unearned opinion reads as noise.
5. **Structure.** Hook that earns the first paragraph, the question, the exploration (his actual reasoning path, cleaned), the landing. No listicle scaffolding unless the content is genuinely enumerable.
6. **Revise with Orwell's six rules** plus his sentence checklist (What am I trying to say? Could I put it more shortly?). Cut hedging -- "I think perhaps it seems" dies here; the essay shelf is for spine.
7. **Ship.** Frontmatter like any chapter; `publishedOn` when he says publish.

## Rules

- His opinions are the content; never soften a take he wants sharp, never sharpen one he hedged deliberately.
- Facts inside essays get checked before publish (a wrong number kills an opinion piece's credibility); flag any claim that needs a source.
- Personal stories stay his call: never suggest adding personal details he didn't offer.
