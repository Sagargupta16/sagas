# Fantasy reader expectations and tropes

Craft rules distilled from live-fetched sources and adversarially source-verified on 2026-07-11. Every attribution below survived a fact-check pass (refuted or corrected claims were fixed before inclusion). Apply as guidance, not dogma: when a rule fights the story, the story wins.

## Sanderson's First Law: magic may only solve problems in proportion to how well the reader understands it

The law verbatim: "An author's ability to solve conflict with magic is DIRECTLY PROPORTIONAL to how well the reader understands said magic." In a hard-magic story, every climax solution must use rules the reader already learned; introducing a new ability at the moment of need reads as cheating. In a soft-magic story (Tolkien-style, rules kept vague for wonder), magic should create problems, not solve them, and the plot should resolve through non-magical means. Practical check before writing a climax: can the reader name the established rule the hero exploits? If not, either seed it earlier or solve the problem without magic.

Source: Brandon Sanderson, "Sanderson's First Law" essay (originally 2007), brandonsanderson.com -- <https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/sandersons-first-law>

## Sanderson's Second Law: limitations are more interesting than powers

Stated as "Limitations > Powers." What a magic user cannot do, what it costs, and what it risks generate more story than the ability itself: Superman is interesting because of kryptonite and his moral code, not flight. Wheel of Time channeling drives male users insane, forcing character choices; Mistborn steelpushing only pushes in straight lines with weight deciding who moves, forcing tactical creativity. Rule for a writing assistant: when designing or extending a power, define at least one cost, one restriction, and one weakness before defining what it enables, and derive conflict from those.

Source: Brandon Sanderson, "Sanderson's Second Law" essay (2012), brandonsanderson.com -- <https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/sandersons-second-law>

## Sanderson's Third Law: expand what you already have before adding something new

Verbatim: "Expand what you already have before you add something new." Depth beats breadth: instead of a new power per book, extrapolate consequences of existing magic (how does it reshape economy, gender roles, war?), interconnect powers under one theme (Mistborn's abilities all map to thieving-crew roles), and streamline (one magic system with ten cultural applications, not ten systems). For a serialized book this is an anti-bloat rule: each arc should deepen established systems and settings rather than bolt on new ones.

Source: Brandon Sanderson, "Sanderson's Third Law of Magic" essay (2013), brandonsanderson.com -- <https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/sandersons-third-law-of-magic>

## Promise, Progress, Payoff: open with a genre promise, signpost progress, pay off what was promised

Sanderson's plot framework: the opening pages promise tone, genre, and the shape of the ending; the middle must give "a sense of satisfying progress" via visible signposts (information revealed, relationships advanced, power gained); the ending must be "a surprising but fulfilling answer to your promise." He warns that "a big reason that people drop off a book is that there weren't enough signposts of progress," and that readers decide within the first chapter whether to continue. For serialized chapters read on phones this compounds: every installment needs its own visible progress marker, and the payoff must match the promise made in chapter one, not a different, cleverer story.

Source: Brandon Sanderson, 2025 BYU lecture series, "Guide to Plot (Lecture #2)", brandonsanderson.com -- <https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/brandon-sandersons-2025-guide-to-plot-lecture-2>

## Trope = yes, stereotype = no: execute tropes, do not discard or shock-subvert them

Tropes (chosen one, secret heir, evil overlord, reluctant hero, mentor, the quest, powerful artifact) are reader expectations, not flaws; the failure mode is the stereotype, an archetype with nothing added. When subverting, the subversion must serve story and character rather than shock value, and twists need planted clues so the reveal feels justified. Worked examples from the article: a chosen one who merely believes they are special, a mentor who leaves so the protagonist must prove themselves, a villain driven by genuine conviction instead of pure evil, and quests justified by escalating stakes.

Source: Reedsy editorial team with quoted professional editors, "The 14 Biggest Fantasy Tropes (and How to Enliven Them)", Reedsy blog -- <https://reedsy.com/blog/fantasy-tropes/>

## Know the current complaint list: default-medieval settings, convenient artifacts, damsels, and trauma-orphan heroes draw fire

A working list of what modern readers flag in reviews: pseudo-European medieval settings used as an unexamined default ("sort of ridiculous" given fantasy's unlimited possibilities), ancient powerful artifacts used as lazy plot devices, side quests that stall momentum, damsels in distress (read as outdated and misogynistic), and orphan heroes that imply trauma is required for greatness. The same article lists tropes readers still love when executed well: chosen ones, reluctant heroes, cavalry arrivals, and antagonist-turned-ally arcs. Rule: before using any of the five flagged tropes, either justify it in-world or replace it; never use one purely as furniture.

Source: Mikkaka Overstreet, "10 of the Best (and Worst) Fantasy Tropes", Book Riot -- <https://www.bookriot.com/best-and-worst-fantasy-tropes/>

## Match the subgenre contract: high/epic fantasy promises a secondary world and epic stakes; low fantasy promises magical intrusion into a real world

High fantasy is set entirely in an invented secondary world and readers expect extensive worldbuilding (maps, races, complex magic systems), epic good-versus-evil quests, battles, long page counts, and many uniquely named characters (Lord of the Rings, Game of Thrones, Shadow and Bone). Low fantasy places magic inside the recognizable real world (urban, historical, paranormal: Harry Potter, Good Omens); it needs no grand quest because the intrusion itself is the hook, and stakes are more personal and grounded. Rule: declare which contract a saga is under at chapter one and do not drift; a low-fantasy opening that pivots into world-ending epic breaks the promise readers signed up for.

Source: N J Simmonds, "High Fantasy Vs Low Fantasy", Jericho Writers -- <https://jerichowriters.com/high-fantasy-vs-low-fantasy/>

## Progression fantasy contract: the Book 3 protagonist must beat the Book 1 protagonist, and power growth must be central and visible

Andrew Rowe, who coined the term with Will Wight in 2019, defines it as fiction "that focuses on characters increasing in power and skill over time," where progression means power specifically, not wealth or status. His practical test: the story qualifies if the Book 3 version of the protagonist could easily defeat the Book 1 version. Readers expect training sequences, technique learning, opponent analysis, and measurable gains, with or without numeric systems (Stormlight Archive qualifies with no game mechanics). Rule for a serial: every arc must end with the protagonist demonstrably stronger or more skilled than the previous arc, shown on the page.

Source: Andrew Rowe (author of Sufficiently Advanced Magic), "Progression Fantasy: A New Subgenre Concept" (2019), personal blog -- <https://andrewkrowe.wordpress.com/2019/02/26/progression-fantasy-a-new-subgenre-concept/>

## LitRPG hard rules: the System is load-bearing, and numbers must be visible and trackable

LitRPG requires explicit game mechanics that both character and reader can track in real time: stat screens, levels, skill points, and in-world systems that actively drive the plot rather than decorate it. This is what separates it from GameLit (game flavor without mandatory stat displays) and progression fantasy (power growth without required mechanics). The genre's contract is legible progress: readers expect regular, quantified advancement (level-ups, skill unlocks, loot) delivered as satisfying beats. Rule: if a saga shows one stat screen, it has promised a system; keep the numbers consistent, keep them appearing, and make the system itself matter to the plot.

Source: LitRPG Critic, "What Is LitRPG? The Definitive Guide to the Genre"; corroborated by LitRPG Audiobook worldbuilding guide -- <https://litrpgcritic.com/blog/2026-03-12-what-is-litrpg-the-definitive-guide-to-the-genre/>

## Escalate up the stakes ladder deliberately and leave headroom for later books

Chris Winkle's stakes ladder runs from negative feelings, to social consequences, to life setbacks, to loss of freedom or health, to danger to lives, to mass destruction of cities or worlds. Raise stakes by making a possible future worse (villain escalates, or the hero learns the situation is worse than believed), but establish solid personal stakes early and deliberately hold back the top rungs: if book one already threatens the whole world, the series has nowhere to go. Stakes that are too low show up as reader boredom; the fix is proximity to what the character values, not just bigger explosions. For power-scaling series, the companion rule from LitRPG craft guides: scale challenges alongside the protagonist so threats stay real as power grows, and blend power fantasy with underdog pressure rather than picking one.

Source: Chris Winkle, "Stakes: Everything Storytellers Need to Know" (2025), Mythcreants; challenge-scaling point from LitRPG Audiobook worldbuilding guide (litrpgaudiobook.com/guides/worldbuilding-tropes) -- <https://mythcreants.com/blog/stakes-everything-storytellers-need-to-know/>

## Conlang-lite naming: pick one sound palette per culture, keep every name pronounceable, and make all names visually distinct

Build a naming language by choosing a limited set of consonants and vowels per culture and never breaking it; consistency is what makes invented names feel real. Keep names pronounceable at a glance (unpronounceable words break reading flow) and avoid the cliche overdose of apostrophes and dashes. Chris Winkle adds concrete guardrails: never lift words from non-Western living cultures as fantasy decoration (reads appropriative), repurposed-English place names like Greenfalls are fine and memorable, and no two important names should share a first letter or a similar shape (Whitefalls vs Greenfalls is asking for confusion, and similar lengths plus vowel sounds fail in audio).

Source: Jessica Lee, "How to Create a Simple Language" (Mythcreants); Chris Winkle, "What Should I Avoid While Creating Names in Fantasy Cultures?" (Mythcreants Q&A, 2021, mythcreants.com/blog/questions/what-should-i-avoid-while-creating-names-in-fantasy-cultures/) -- <https://mythcreants.com/blog/how-to-create-a-simple-language/>

## Budget exposition hard: no 8,000-word lore scenes; drip-feed the system the way Mistborn does

Info dumping is the most consistent fantasy reader complaint, and Oren Ashkenazi puts a number on it: an approximately 8,000-word exposition conversation is "almost certainly too much to give at once," citing readers who abandoned A Deadly Education over its 19-page opening dump. Readers can only absorb so much at a time, especially rules they must retain for later payoff. The prescribed fix: structure the plot so protagonist and reader learn the magic gradually across chapters (his named model is Sanderson's Mistborn, where Vin's training doles the system out scene by scene), and if the system still cannot be taught gradually, simplify the system. Relatedly, his manuscript-editing checklist of worldbuilding complaints includes implausibly absent authorities and unaddressed world-scale crises (mythcreants.com/blog/five-common-worldbuilding-mistakes-in-new-manuscripts/), both of which read to modern audiences as unreal worlds.

Source: Oren Ashkenazi, "How Much Exposition Can I Have?" (Mythcreants Q&A, 2023) -- <https://mythcreants.com/blog/questions/how-much-exposition-can-i-have/>

## Caveats

1. Sanderson explicitly frames his Laws as guidelines for his own hard-magic style, not universal rules: the First Law essay validates Tolkien-style soft magic as equally legitimate, so "explain your magic" is NOT a genre-wide requirement, only a payoff requirement once hard-magic promises are made. 2) "Avoid the chosen one" is contested: Book Riot lists the chosen one among the BEST tropes and Reedsy's editors say tropes are reader expectations where only stereotype-level execution fails; the widely repeated claim that tropes must be subverted is not consensus advice. 3) The LitRPG Critic guide's statistics (35% higher series completion, 28% higher retention at Book 5) are presented with no methodology and could not be verified; treat the definitional content as consensus but the numbers as unverified marketing. 4) Progression fantasy is a young subgenre (term coined 2019 by Rowe and Wight) and its conventions are still stabilizing; the Rowe post is a proposal that the community largely adopted, not a settled canon. 5) Preferred sources MasterClass and Writer's Digest returned 403/404 and could not be used; Mythcreants pages blocked WebFetch (403) and were fetched via curl with a browser user agent, content read in full. 6) The Reddit thread on late-series bloat (Wheel of Time / ASOIAF editor-pushback theories) could not be fetched, so that specific complaint pattern is represented indirectly via the Mythcreants exposition and stakes sources.
