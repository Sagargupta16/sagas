# Openings, hooks, and endings

Craft rules distilled from live-fetched sources and adversarially source-verified on 2026-07-11. Every attribution below survived a fact-check pass (refuted or corrected claims were fixed before inclusion). Apply as guidance, not dogma: when a rule fights the story, the story wins.

## Never open with weather; avoid prologues (Leonard's rules 1 and 2)

Elmore Leonard's first two rules from his 2001 New York Times essay: 'Never open a book with weather' -- unless the weather is filtered through a character's reaction, readers skip ahead looking for people. 'Avoid prologues' -- they are backstory, and backstory can be dropped in anywhere else. His umbrella rule covers both: 'Try to leave out the part that readers tend to skip,' and 'If it sounds like writing, I rewrite it.' He explicitly names exceptions (Barry Lopez can open with weather), so treat these as defaults, not laws.

Source: Elmore Leonard, 'Easy on the Adverbs, Exclamation Points and Especially Hooptedoodle', The New York Times (2001-07-16), later the book Elmore Leonard's 10 Rules of Writing -- <https://www.themarginalian.org/2013/08/21/elmore-leonard-10-rules-of-writing/>

## The first line is an invitation in the book's voice: 'Listen. Come in here.'

Stephen King says 'An opening line should invite the reader to begin the story. It should say: Listen. Come in here. You want to know about this.' He drafts opening paragraphs in bed and rewords them 'over a period of weeks and months and even years' because the line must establish voice -- 'readers come for the voice,' not the plot or genre. Practical test: read the first line aloud; it should sound like a person confiding something, not a report. His models are ultra-plain confiding lines: 'They threw me off the hay truck about noon' (Cain) and 'This is what happened' (Fairbairn's Shoot).

Source: Stephen King, interview with Joe Fassler, The Atlantic 'By Heart' series (2013), 'Why Stephen King Spends Months and Even Years Writing Opening Sentences' -- <https://www.yahoo.com/news/why-stephen-king-spends-months-even-years-writing-180500819.html>

## Start as close to the end as possible

Rule 5 of Kurt Vonnegut's eight rules for fiction: begin the story at the latest possible moment that still lets the ending land, cutting run-up and throat-clearing. Pairs with his rule 8: 'Give your readers as much information as possible as soon as possible. To heck with suspense' -- clarity hooks better than withholding. For a serialized chapter, this means opening each installment at the last defensible moment before the chapter's key turn. Vonnegut himself flags that great writers (Flannery O'Connor) break most of these rules.

Source: Kurt Vonnegut, introduction to Bagombo Snuff Box (1999) -- <https://www.openculture.com/2015/04/kurt-vonneguts-8-tips-on-how-to-write-a-good-short-story.html>

## The prologue question is really a promises question: start small, promise big

Brandon Sanderson's answer to 'do I need a prologue?': 'you certainly do not need them. In fact, many authors use them as a crutch,' and some editors advise cutting them entirely. What matters is not the label on the opening but 'making certain your opening is making the right kinds of promises' -- you can open with something modest (a foot race, a bold claim) that foreshadows epic scope. Write the book first, then rebuild the opening in revision to match what the story became. Keep a prologue only if it makes a promise chapter one cannot.

Source: Brandon Sanderson, 'FAQ Friday: Do I need to start my book with a prologue?', brandonsanderson.com -- <https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/faq-friday-do-i-need-to-start-my-book-with-a-prologue>

## Chapter one makes four promises: tone, story, character/conflict, structure

In his 2025 plot lecture Sanderson breaks the opening's job into four promise types -- tone (what mood/experience this book delivers), story (direction and stakes), character and conflict (who the protagonist is and what they want against what obstacle), and structural/genre promises that orient the reader. 'The first chapter and first few pages are when people will decide whether or not they're going to put your book down.' Actionable check for any chapter one: name the promise of each type it makes; a missing promise type is a revision target.

Source: Brandon Sanderson, 2025 BYU writing lecture series, 'Guide to Plot (Lecture #2)', brandonsanderson.com -- <https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/brandon-sandersons-2025-guide-to-plot-lecture-2>

## In medias res: enter late, leave early, and know the whole scene before you cut into it

Reedsy's guide defines in medias res as opening mid-action and back-filling through dialogue or flashback (versus ab ovo, from the beginning). It works by raising immediate questions the reader must read on to answer. The concrete method: plan the chronological events first, write the full pivotal scene, then 'enter late and leave early' -- delete the explanatory head and tail; decide up front how withheld context will be delivered. The failure mode is confusion, so pair the dropped-in action with fast grounding of who/where.

Source: Reedsy Blog, 'In Medias Res: Definition, Usage, and Examples' -- <https://reedsy.com/blog/in-medias-res/>

## Kill the four opening pitfalls: false suspense, info-dump prologues, dream openings, front-loaded backstory

K.M. Weiland's four biggest opening pitfalls: (1) false suspense -- confusing readers about who/where/when is not tension; ground name, age, place, and relationships fast so the questions readers ask are plot questions; (2) prologues that exist to deliver backstory force readers to invest twice; (3) dream openings lack real stakes and break trust; (4) backstory lands harder AFTER the reader cares -- show its effects and distribute the past through the narrative. Rule of thumb: the reader should be confused about what happens next, never about what is happening now.

Source: K.M. Weiland, '4 Big Pitfalls in Story Openings', guest post on Jane Friedman's blog -- <https://www.janefriedman.com/pitfalls-in-story-openings/>

## Ban the cliche opener list -- especially waking up and alarm clocks -- and write the first chapter last

Anne R. Allen's list of openers agents auto-reject: weather reports, travel scenes, funerals, dreams, 'Hello, my name is' intros, mirror self-descriptions, crowd scenes, pure internal musing, action before any character is established, and the alarm-clock wake-up. They fail because they are overused and delay actual story engagement before stakes exist. Her fix: draft freely, then 'write the first chapter last' -- rebuild the opening after the manuscript exists; if you must use a stock opener, subvert it hard. This is a checkable lint rule for an AI assistant: flag any chapter one whose first scene matches the list.

Source: Anne R. Allen (mystery author, Writer's Digest contributor), 'How to start your novel or memoir: 11 Cliched Openers to avoid' (2021) -- <https://annerallen.com/2021/08/11-cliched-openers-novel-memoir/>

## Open with a question only reading on can answer; a smolder beats a bang

Reedsy's editor-sourced guide to starting a story: pose an implicit question the reader can only resolve by continuing; use short, sparse opening sentences that make readers lean in; ground the scene in specific sensory setting before exposition. Editor Rebecca Heyman warns against opening with a literal bang -- explosive chaos before the reader knows anyone keeps them at a distance; controlled, smoldering tension retains better. Editor Nathan Connolly's framing: the opening should create a wound only reading on can heal.

Source: Reedsy Blog, 'How to Start a Story' (advice from editors Nathan Connolly, Rebecca Heyman, Anne McPeak) -- <https://reedsy.com/blog/how-to-start-a-story/>

## Pick the ending type deliberately: resolved, unresolved, ambiguous, unexpected, tied, or expanded

Reedsy's taxonomy of six ending types with canonical examples: resolved (all threads closed; One Hundred Years of Solitude), unresolved (doors left open for the next installment; Half-Blood Prince), ambiguous (interpretation left open; The Giver), unexpected/twist (must be seeded so it feels earned; And Then There Were None), tied (return to the opening image; The Night Circus), and expanded (epilogue glimpse of the future; The Book Thief). Choice rules: standalone books resolve the central question; series installments resolve the book's arc while leaving series threads live; genre sets constraints (romance wants HEA, mystery must name the killer). Also from this guide: last lines outlast first lines in reader memory, and the final image should echo the theme.

Source: Reedsy Blog, 'How to End a Story: 6 Types of Endings' -- <https://reedsy.com/blog/how-to-end-a-story/>

## The ending is a social contract: pay off the opening's promises, surprising yet inevitable, and don't overstay

Writing Excuses 6.20 (Sanderson, Wells, Kowal, Tayler with Lou Anders): 'the groundwork and promises of the beginning are a social contract fulfilled by the ending' -- most bad endings are actually setup failures earlier in the book. Sanderson's lecture version: a payoff is 'a surprising but fulfilling answer to your promise,' unexpected AND inevitable, and it does not require a twist. Anders' Hollywood Formula: land the antagonist defeat, goal achievement, and relationship reconciliation as close together as possible for maximum emotional impact. Two more rules from the episode: escalate stakes by making them more personal, not more apocalyptic; and cut the paragraphs after the emotional peak -- overstaying undermines the landing.

Source: Writing Excuses 6.20 'Endings' (Dan Wells, Howard Tayler, Mary Robinette Kowal, guest Lou Anders) -- <https://writingexcuses.com/writing-excuses-6-20-endings/>

## Epilogues are a bonus, not a bandage: one short chapter, no info-dump, seed sequels earlier

Reedsy's epilogue guide: an epilogue lives inside the story (unlike an afterword, which is about the book) but outside the main narrative frame, after the denouement. Use it only for what the ending genuinely could not tidy -- 'make sure you aren't using your epilogue to beat a dead horse, or as a band-aid to patch up holes in your story.' Concrete limits: about the length of a short chapter (roughly 5-8 pages), no over-explaining every character's fate, and if it exists to set up a sequel, the hook must be seeded earlier so it does not read as a bolted-on ad. The main ending must stand without it.

Source: Reedsy Blog, 'What is the Epilogue of a Book?' -- <https://reedsy.com/blog/guide/parts-of-a-book/epilogue/>

## End a series when the characters run out of room to grow, and track promises across books

From the Alliance of Independent Authors' guide to finishing a series: the end-signal is character-arc exhaustion -- 'when the characters have overcome their initial flaws/neuroses and there's not much room for them to grow or change, then it's time to wind things down' (Kassandra Lamb). For sticking a multi-book landing, Alison Morton tracks the whole-series arc mechanically (spreadsheets of threads per book) so the finale can resolve promises made volumes earlier. For serialized web fiction this translates to: keep a living promises ledger per book and per series, and check the finale against it.

Source: Alliance of Independent Authors (selfpublishingadvice.org), 'The Ultimate Guide to Finishing a Series', quoting authors Kassandra Lamb and Alison Morton -- <https://selfpublishingadvice.org/the-ultimate-guide-to-finishing-a-series/>

## Caveats

Primary-source access limits: theguardian.com, theatlantic.com, and web.archive.org are blocked by the fetch tool, and masterclass.com / writersdigest.com returned 403/DNS errors, so Leonard's NYT rules were verified via The Marginalian's full quotation and King's Atlantic interview via Yahoo News's licensed repost plus Goodreads/Open Culture cross-checks; the wording matches across sources. Contested rules: (1) Leonard's rules are deliberately tongue-in-cheek and he names exceptions in the essay itself (Barry Lopez may open with weather); treat as strong defaults for lean prose, not universals -- Novlr and others publish critiques of over-applying them. (2) The anti-prologue rule is genuinely contested in fantasy: agents/editors (Weiland, some of Sanderson's editors) advise cutting, while Sanderson reframes it as a promises problem and epic fantasy routinely uses prologues successfully (his own Way of Kings has two); the actionable synthesis is 'default to none; keep only if it makes a promise chapter one cannot.' (3) Vonnegut prefixes his 8 rules by noting Flannery O'Connor broke almost all of them. (4) Reedsy's claim that final lines matter more than opening lines for lasting impression is editorial opinion, not measured fact. (5) The series-ending finding rests on indie-author testimony (ALLi) rather than a marquee author interview; Writing Excuses episode 6.20 covers book endings, not series finales specifically. In medias res is classically attributed to Horace's Ars Poetica; the fetched Reedsy page teaches the technique but the Horace attribution itself was not verified from a primary classical source.
