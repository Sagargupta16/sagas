# Exposition and backstory

Craft rules distilled from live-fetched sources and adversarially source-verified on 2026-07-11. Every attribution below survived a fact-check pass (refuted or corrected claims were fixed before inclusion). Apply as guidance, not dogma: when a rule fights the story, the story wins.

## Inclue, don't explain: scatter world facts seamlessly through scenes

Jo Walton coined 'incluing' as 'the process of scattering information seamlessly through the text, as opposed to stopping the story to impart the information.' The practical move: never halt the narrative to lecture; instead distribute each lore fact into dialogue, character thought, background detail, or in-universe media, so the reader assembles the big picture across chapters. This is the positive counterpart to the info-dump prohibition: for every fact you cut from a dump, find a scene where it can surface as a side effect of something a character is doing.

Source: Jo Walton (term and definition), documented in Wikipedia's 'Incluing' article citing her papersky.livejournal.com post and the rec.arts.sf.composition FAQ -- <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incluing>

## The casual-detail drop: mention the strange as if it were normal

Indirect exposition, pioneered by Kipling and refined by Heinlein (the canonical example is Heinlein's unexplained 'the door dilated'), conveys worldbuilding by having the narrator or characters treat exotic elements as unremarkable furniture. Rule for the assistant: introduce an invented technology, custom, or magic term in passing, attached to an action, and do NOT define it on first use; let context over the next few pages carry the meaning. The corollary prohibition is the 'idiot lecture,' dialogue where characters explain things they would already know ('As you well know, Professor, a prime number is...').

Source: Robert A. Heinlein's practice as described in Wikipedia's 'Exposition (narrative)' article (which credits Kipling as pioneer and Heinlein as refiner) -- <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exposition_(narrative)>

## Exposition as ammunition: deliver facts inside a fight

Robert McKee's rule from Dialogue (pp. 28-29): 'at pivotal moments, let your characters use what they know as ammunition in their struggles to get what they want.' Backstory lands when a character weaponizes it, revealing a secret to win an argument, extract a concession, or wound someone, because 'these revelations will deliver the pleasure of discovery to the emotionally invested reader/audience as the fact quickly vanishes into the story-goer's background awareness.' Assistant application: before writing any explanatory dialogue, give one character a motive to reveal the fact and another a reason to resist hearing it.

Source: Robert McKee, Dialogue: The Art of Verbal Action for the Page, Stage, and Screen (2016), quoted in a Medium close-reading of pp. 28-29 -- <https://medium.com/@pirangy/exposition-as-ammunition-robert-mckee-dialogue-the-art-of-verbal-action-for-the-page-stage-and-e9b38a4d2361>

## The Le Guin necessity test, then smuggle what survives

Le Guin's two-step for the 'Expository Lump or the Infodump': first decide (or find out when revising) 'whether the information is actually necessary. If not, don't bother.' If it is, 'figure out how to work it in as a functional, forward-moving element of the story, enriching scenery and character while informing the reader,' preferably 'indirectly, by hint and suggestion.' She also names the drafting reality: early drafts are full of 'Department of Fuller Explanation' material the writer needed but the reader won't; the fix is a dedicated revision pass that cuts or relocates it 'more sneakily.'

Source: Ursula K. Le Guin, 'Navigating the Ocean of Story' online workshop (Book View Cafe), published on her official site -- <https://www.ursulakleguin.com/bvc-navigating-the-ocean-of-story-session-1-continued>

## Flashback gate: the act of remembering must be a plot catalyst

K.M. Weiland's test: a flashback is justified only when the backstory is interesting AND moves the present plot, 'the very act of the character's remembering must be a plot catalyst.' Her three failure signs: the flashback is 'bigger' than the information it conveys (don't spend a scene to deliver one fact); it destroys subtext ('The moment you bring that backstory into the clear light of dramatized narrative, it ceases to be subtext'); and it steals emphasis from the present-day narrative. Assistant application: if the same fact can be implied in one line of present-scene dialogue, cut the flashback.

Source: K.M. Weiland, 'The Only Reason Your Story Should Have Flashbacks,' Helping Writers Become Authors -- <https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/reason-story-flashbacks/>

## Flashback mechanics: enter on past perfect, settle into simple past, reverse to exit

Nancy Kress's craft rules: (1) a flashback should follow a strong present-time scene and 'is never the first scene'; (2) orient the reader in time and place in the flashback's first lines; (3) in a past-tense narrative, use past perfect ('had done') for the first few verbs to signal descent into memory, shift to simple past for the body, then return to past perfect briefly on exit; in a present-tense narrative, put the whole flashback in past tense. She adds the justification test: a flashback earns its place when the essential event happened too long ago to stage otherwise, or when it reveals motivation or how the situation arose.

Source: Nancy Kress, '3 Tips for Writing Successful Flashbacks,' Writer's Digest -- <https://www.writersdigest.com/writing-articles/3-tips-for-writing-successful-flashbacks>

## The maid-and-butler trap: dialogue that serves the reader but not the speakers

Beyond As-You-Know-Bob, editor Richard Bradburn names 'maid and butler' dialogue (rooted in Greek-drama prologues via Euripides): two characters discuss background both already know, purely to brief the audience. His fix: 'When you have something important for a character to say, make sure that they say it in a natural way, not in a declamatory, blunt fashion,' with natural hesitations, interruptions, and disagreement, and accept that showing 'generally takes a higher word-count than telling.' Assistant check: for every expository line of dialogue, ask 'would this speaker say this to this listener if no reader existed?'

Source: Richard Bradburn, 'Writing Dialogue' craft post, Alliance of Independent Authors Self-Publishing Advice Center -- <https://selfpublishingadvice.org/writing-dialogue/>

## Three good details beat the police-artist sketch

Patrick Rothfuss on Writing Excuses: dumping '50 details and a half a page later, the reader has forgotten everything'; instead pick 'three good details' and cut the rest. The same episode names the other bad shapes (the essay, the thesis-statement open, starting with an info-dump) and the positive moves: open with dialogue or action, reveal the world 'through the characters interacting with it,' and, per Vonnegut, make 'every sentence do more than one thing' so exposition doubles as characterization or humor.

Source: Patrick Rothfuss with Brandon Sanderson, Dan Wells, Howard Tayler, Writing Excuses episode 32, 'Talking Exposition with Patrick Rothfuss' -- <https://writingexcuses.com/writing-excuses-episodes-32-talking-exposition-with-patrick-rothfuss/>

## Tease then withhold: make explanation a payoff, not a burden

Rothfuss's positive lore-delivery loop: 'give the readers a little -- tease them -- then withhold the rest,' so that when the full explanation finally arrives 'it is a payoff not a burden.' The companion trick is misdirection through selective emphasis: casually over-explain unimportant details while leaving the significant elements mysterious, which trains readers to lean in. For a serialized book this maps directly to chapter design: raise the lore question in one chapter, pay it off one or more chapters later.

Source: Patrick Rothfuss, Writing Excuses episode 32, 'Talking Exposition with Patrick Rothfuss' -- <https://writingexcuses.com/writing-excuses-episodes-32-talking-exposition-with-patrick-rothfuss/>

## Know the named failure modes AND the two named successes: kuttnering and heinleining

The Turkey City Lexicon (SF workshop shorthand, compiled by Lewis Shiner, 2nd ed. Bruce Sterling) defines the exposition failure family: 'As You Know Bob' (characters telling each other what they already know), 'Info-dump' (large undigested expository blocks), 'I've Suffered for My Art' (inflicting accurate-but-irrelevant research on readers, per Algis Budrys), 'Frontloading' (dense exposition concentrated at the opening), and 'Nowhere Nowhen Story' (the opposite defect: so little backstory the story floats in a vacuum). Crucially it also names the good variants: 'kuttnering' (brief, skillfully-handled info-dumps, after Henry Kuttner) and 'heinleining' (information woven invisibly into the narrative), so an assistant can prescribe, not just prohibit.

Source: Turkey City Lexicon, ed. Lewis Shiner and Bruce Sterling, published by SFWA -- <https://sfwa.org/2009/06/18/turkey-city-lexicon-a-primer-for-sf-workshops/>

## Choose the delivery channel deliberately: the four modes of exposition

Reedsy's taxonomy gives the assistant a decision menu: (1) direct exposition, plain narrator statement, which is legitimate when the topic is genuinely complex ('The more complex a topic, the more difficult it is to show it elegantly'); (2) indirect exposition via context clues (Emily St. John Mandel implies time travel by having a character mention Covid-19 in January 2020); (3) dialogue exposition embedded in a real conflict (Fourth Wing reveals Violet's fragility through a family argument, not a narrator aside); (4) exposition through action, where behavior carries the backstory (Alice 'crept around' her husband's house in The Giver of Stars). Rule: pick the mode per fact; default to 2-4, reserve 1 for genuinely complex mechanics.

Source: Reedsy blog craft guide 'Exposition,' citing Emily St. John Mandel, Rebecca Yarros, Jojo Moyes, Gary Stevenson -- <https://reedsy.com/blog/exposition/>

## Depth over density: deepen few elements, imply the rest (iceberg)

Sanderson's 2025 worldbuilding lecture writeup: 'It's not about density of ideas. It's about how the ideas influence each other and the depth that you can play with those ideas.' Keep most of the world below the waterline; surface only elements that touch character arcs and plot, and anchor abstract lore in concrete sensory experience (e.g., the metallic taste of burning metals in Mistborn) so readers infer the deeper rules organically instead of receiving 'encyclopedia-style info dumps.' He also warns against worldbuilder's disease: building lore you will then feel compelled to dump.

Source: Brandon Sanderson, 'Worldbuilding Tools' 2025 lecture writeup on his official site -- <https://brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/worldbuilding-tools-lecture-2025>

## Caveats

Attribution notes: (1) 'Incluing' is Jo Walton's coinage but her original definition survives mainly via her LiveJournal (now unfetchable) and the rec.arts.sf.composition FAQ; Wikipedia is the most stable citation. (2) The Turkey City Lexicon is collective workshop folklore, Shiner/Sterling edited it, individual entries mostly have no single author (the 'I've Suffered for My Art' note is credited to Algis Budrys). (3) The punchier McKee line often quoted ('no fact may be delivered without a fight' / 'fired in the middle of a scene where someone is fighting') appeared only in search-result snippets of secondary sites I could not fully fetch; the quote I sourced directly is the softer Dialogue pp. 28-29 passage via a Medium close-reading, i.e. near-primary, not the book itself. (4) Kress's 'a flashback is never the first scene' is her rule of thumb, not consensus; plenty of published novels open in the past, treat it as a strong default rather than a law. (5) 'Show, don't tell' as a blanket rule is contested: Reedsy and Le Guin both defend direct telling for complex material, so the skills should frame telling as a legitimate mode, not a sin. (6) The Sanderson lecture page was fetched via an AI-summarizing fetcher; short quotes are as returned but the 'Pyramid of Abstraction' framing may be partly the summarizer's paraphrase of his lecture language. (7) Several canonical pages (SFWA lexicon on first try, TV Tropes, Writer Unboxed, Jo Walton's reactormag essays) intermittently 403'd; where possible I re-fetched or used mirrors, but reactormag/tor.com content could not be reached at all from this environment.
