# Revision and self-editing

Craft rules distilled from live-fetched sources and adversarially source-verified on 2026-07-11. Every attribution below survived a fact-check pass (refuted or corrected claims were fixed before inclusion). Apply as guidance, not dogma: when a rule fights the story, the story wins.

## Drawer time: rest the finished draft (King says minimum six weeks)

When the first draft is done, put it away and do not reread it until you can see it as a stranger would. King's exact line: "How long you let your book rest -- sort of like bread dough between kneadings -- is entirely up to you, but I think it should be a minimum of six weeks." For a serialized project, apply this at chapter scale: never publish the same day you draft; let a chapter cool at least overnight (ideally days) before the revision pass. The point is objectivity, not the exact number of weeks.

Source: Stephen King, On Writing: A Memoir of the Craft (2000), as excerpted in Nat Eliason's book notes -- <https://nateliason.com/notes/writing-stephen-king>

## 2nd Draft = 1st Draft - 10%

King's revision formula: the second draft should be at least ten percent shorter than the first. The Eliason notes quote it verbatim: "Formula: 2nd Draft = 1st Draft - 10%." The formula came to King on a rejection slip early in his career (feedback that his work had potential but was "too fluffy"), and he says he has lived by it since. Actionable form: after the story-level pass, run a cutting pass with a numeric target; adverbs, explanations, and redundant description are the first candidates.

Source: Stephen King, On Writing (2000); origin anecdote of the rejection-slip formula covered by Elizabeth Beeli (https://elizabethbeeli.com/2023/01/31/stephen-kings-writing-formula/) -- <https://nateliason.com/notes/writing-stephen-king>

## Write with the door closed, rewrite with the door open (for your Ideal Reader)

King urges at least two drafts: "the one you do with the study door closed and the one you do with it open." Draft one is private and judgment-free; revision is where you consider the audience. His mechanism for the open door is the Ideal Reader: "every novelist has a single ideal reader... the writer is thinking, 'I wonder what he/she will think when he/she reads this part?'" Actionable form: during revision, evaluate every scene against one specific imagined reader's reaction (bored here? confused here?), not a vague crowd.

Source: Stephen King, On Writing (2000), as excerpted in Nat Eliason's book notes -- <https://nateliason.com/notes/writing-stephen-king>

## Revise macro-to-micro: story pass, then scene pass, then line pass, then copyedit

Reedsy's editing guide orders the passes: big-picture issues (plot, structure, character) first, then conflict/theme, then scene-by-scene elements (scenes, dialogue, prose), then sentence-level copy editing, then beta readers, then a professional editor. Its core rule: "Don't try to fix everything at the same time" -- run multiple rounds that each look at different elements, because line-polishing a scene you may later delete is wasted work. Its copyediting checklist is directly automatable: active voice, no adverbs in dialogue tags, stronger verbs, delete hidden verbs, remove crutch words like "really"/"maybe", cut cliches, check dialogue formatting, catch head-hopping, catch descriptive inconsistencies.

Source: Reedsy, "How to Edit a Book" (Reedsy blog editing guide) -- <https://reedsy.com/blog/how-to-edit-a-book/>

## Separate editing from proofreading, and hunt one error type at a time

The UNC Writing Center distinguishes editing (content, structure, clarity -- "what you begin doing as soon as you finish your first draft") from proofreading (surface errors: spelling, grammar, punctuation) and insists they be kept as separate stages in that order. Its concrete proofreading rules: read slowly and read every word, work from a printout or changed format, and "proofread for only one kind of error at a time" -- a systematic sweep beats a single catch-all read. This maps cleanly to running separate targeted passes (typos pass, punctuation pass, consistency pass) rather than one general one.

Source: The Writing Center, University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, "Editing and Proofreading" handout -- <https://writingcenter.unc.edu/tips-and-tools/editing-and-proofreading/>

## Read the chapter aloud before publishing

UNC's handout: reading aloud "forces you to say each word and also lets you hear how the words sound together," catching clunky rhythm, awkward constructions, and errors the eye skips. Browne and King apply the same test specifically to dialogue: "The best way to fine-tune the rhythm of your dialogue... is to read it aloud. Listen for the pauses as you read, and if you find yourself pausing between two consecutive lines, consider inserting a beat." Actionable form: a read-aloud (or text-to-speech) pass is the final gate before a chapter ships; anywhere you stumble, rewrite.

Source: UNC Writing Center, "Editing and Proofreading"; dialogue application from Renni Browne & Dave King, Self-Editing for Fiction Writers, as quoted by Craft Better Books (https://www.craftbetterbooks.com/blog/recommended-resource-self-editing) -- <https://writingcenter.unc.edu/tips-and-tools/editing-and-proofreading/>

## R.U.E. -- Resist the Urge to Explain (Browne & King's flagship check)

Cut any sentence that tells the reader what the dialogue or action just showed. The canonical symptom is the adverb-laden dialogue tag: in "'I'm sorry,' Tom said apologetically," the adverb duplicates the line itself -- "Those adverbs are usually signals that you're telling something the dialogue should be showing." The same applies to naming an emotion next to an action that demonstrates it ("Mary was depressed. She took one bite of chocolate cake, then pushed the plate away" -- delete the first sentence). Guiding principle from the same tradition: "Readers are smart. Don't patronize them." Editing checks derived from the book: default to "said," no said-bookisms, replace tag adverbs with action beats, and delete explanation sentences that restate shown content.

Source: Renni Browne & Dave King, Self-Editing for Fiction Writers (2nd ed. 2004), as explained by editor blog The Blood-Red Pencil and Terry Odell at Pikes Peak Writers (https://pikespeakwriters.blogspot.com/2017/01/resist-urge-to-explain-another-take.html) -- <https://bloodredpencil.blogspot.com/2014/12/resist-urge-to-explain.html>

## Strip filter words to close narrative distance

Filter words (saw, felt, heard, noticed, seemed, realized, thought, wondered, knew, decided, spotted, believed) mediate the character's experience through a reporting verb. Harnby: "Filter words are verbs that increase the narrative distance, reminding us that what we're reading is being told by someone rather than experienced," and when habitual they "tap the reader on the shoulder and say, 'You're not in this book.'" Fix by deleting the filter and rendering the perception directly: "When he saw that the dark had mellowed, he shuffled inside" becomes "When the dark mellowed, he shuffled inside." Exception: keep a filter word when the noticing itself is the point, or for deliberate pull-back and voice.

Source: Louise Harnby (professional fiction editor), "Filter words in fiction: purposeful inclusion and dramatic restriction" -- <https://www.louiseharnbyproofreader.com/blog/filter-words-in-fiction-purposeful-inclusion-and-dramatic-restriction>

## Run a crutch-word hunt with a personal watchlist

Every writer has unconscious filler words (common offenders: just, quite, really, literally, very, actually, basically, suddenly, felt, look). Workflow from Novlr: identify your repeated words by comparing samples of your own writing, highlight/search for them, then in revision cut them or replace weak qualifier+adjective pairs with one specific word ("really good" -> "breathtaking"). "Eliminating those words is exactly what revision is for." One stated exception: crutch words in dialogue can be intentional character voice. For a repo-based serial, this is a mechanical pass: maintain the author's watchlist in the project and grep each chapter before publish.

Source: Novlr, "5 crutch words to eliminate from your writing" -- <https://www.novlr.org/the-reading-room/5-crutch-words-to-eliminate-from-your-writing>

## Beta readers come after self-editing and before (not instead of) an editor; act on patterns, not individual notes

Reedsy's ordering: "First, you take your manuscript through several rounds of self-editing; when you're happy with it, you share it with a few beta readers... with as much of the revision work completed as possible, you submit it to an editor." Betas give a reader's subjective reaction; a developmental editor gives trained structural advice -- "a beta reader can't replace a developmental editor." The triage rule for feedback: "look for patterns. If three people say the same scene feels off, that's worth looking at" -- a single opinion is data, a repeated one is a defect. In an open serial, chapter comments effectively serve as rolling beta feedback and should be triaged the same way.

Source: Reedsy, "What is a beta reader?" guide -- <https://reedsy.com/blog/beta-readers/>

## Sanderson's draft ladder: draft 2 fixes major problems, draft 3 is a language pass cutting ~10%, readers only see polish

Sanderson (primary source, his own FAQ): draft one gets things in place ("Draft one, hopefully no one ever sees"); draft two is "read through the entire book, fixing the major problems" and unifying character voices; draft three is "the language draft," where he "seek[s] to cut the book down by 10%" for vibrancy and pacing -- only then do alpha readers, editor, and community beta readers see it, and feedback is compiled into revision notes sorted by importance. All told it is "at least eight to twelve times through the book before it's published." Actionable rules: separate the problem-fixing pass from the language pass, keep a written problems list, and never publish a first draft.

Source: Brandon Sanderson, official FAQ answers "What is your process for drafting a book?" and "When do you send a novel to your editor?" (https://faq.brandonsanderson.com/knowledge-base/when-do-you-send-a-novel-to-your-editor/) -- <https://faq.brandonsanderson.com/knowledge-base/what-is-your-process-for-drafting-a-book/>

## In a public serial, fix typos and small continuity errors in place; fix story problems forward, never by silently rewriting the past

Once chapters are published, silent structural rewrites damage reader trust: readers hold different versions and it "kind of feels like cheating." The recommended split: minor mechanical fixes (typos, punctuation, a wrong name) are safe to patch in place; real continuity or story mistakes should be owned and repaired in new chapters -- "we should own that mistake and try and fix it in the present instead of rewriting the past" -- e.g. reframing the error through another character's viewpoint, and any in-place update should be announced transparently in author notes "to make sure your readers don't feel hoodwinked." Royal Road community threads reflect the same consensus (minor corrections yes, major alterations no); serial authors like Wildbow explicitly defer large-scale rewriting to a separate published edition. For a git-backed serial this maps naturally: typo commits to published chapters are fine, plot changes get a changelog note or go into future chapters.

Source: Hidden Gems Books (author-services blog), "Use Retconning to Fix Your Series Without Losing Readers"; corroborated by Royal Road forum consensus surfaced in search snippets (threads 153373, 160129, 135240) -- <https://www.hiddengemsbooks.com/use-retconning-to-fix-series/>

## Caveats

(1) "Kill your darlings" is routinely credited to Stephen King or William Faulkner, but the original is Arthur Quiller-Couch: "Whenever you feel an impulse to perpetrate a piece of exceptionally fine writing, obey it -- whole-heartedly -- and delete it before sending your manuscript to press. Murder your darlings," from On the Art of Writing (Cambridge lectures, 1913-1914), verified via Wikiquote, which flags the misattribution; King's On Writing popularized a paraphrase. (2) The "2nd Draft = 1st Draft - 10%" formula was not invented by King -- per his own telling it came from an editor's rejection slip; King adopted and popularized it. (3) Several first-choice primary pages were blocked or unreachable during research (The Paris Review King interview returned 403, MasterClass articles 403, Shortform 404, mythcreants 403, King's six-weeks quote therefore comes from reputable secondary excerpts of On Writing (Nat Eliason's notes, corroborated by two other sites quoting the identical "bread dough between kneadings" passage), and Browne & King's rules come from editor blogs and a craft-resource site quoting the book rather than the book text itself. (4) Royal Road forum threads on editing published chapters could not be fetched directly (403/DNS); the serial-revision finding rests on the fetched Hidden Gems article plus search-level snippets of those threads, so the "community consensus" framing is directional rather than quoted. (5) King's six weeks and 10% are stated as personal practice/heuristics, not universal laws -- King himself says rest length "is entirely up to you"; treat the numbers as defaults, not rules.
