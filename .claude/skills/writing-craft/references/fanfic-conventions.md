# Fan fiction craft and conventions

Craft rules distilled from live-fetched sources and adversarially source-verified on 2026-07-11. Every attribution below survived a fact-check pass (refuted or corrected claims were fixed before inclusion). Apply as guidance, not dogma: when a rule fights the story, the story wins.

## One concept per tag, in the correct field, canonical when possible

AO3's own tagging guidance: put fandom names in the Fandoms field, pairings in Relationships, character names in Characters, and everything else in Additional Tags. Each tag must contain exactly one concept and must stand alone without extra context (use full character names, since tags are shared site-wide -- 'Hiro' alone can never be wrangled to Hiro Hamada). Prefer canonical tags from the autocomplete so the work appears in filters immediately; use Additional Tags for tropes, genre, when the work takes place relative to canon, and content notes. Separate tags with commas or Enter, and spell-check before posting.

Source: Archive of Our Own, Tags FAQ (Organization for Transformative Works) -- <https://archiveofourown.org/faq/tags>

## Warn for the Big Four or explicitly opt out; never leave content warnings implicit

AO3 requires creators to either warn for, or explicitly decline to warn for, four archive warnings: Graphic Depictions of Violence, Major Character Death, Rape/Non-Con, and Underage. 'Creator Chose Not To Use Archive Warnings' exists specifically so authors can avoid spoilers without lying to readers; 'No Archive Warnings Apply' is an affirmative claim. Note also that 'Not Rated' is treated like Mature/Explicit by the archive's screening, so unrated is not a neutral default. For a serialized library, adopt the same contract: every chapter or book front-matter declares warnings or an explicit opt-out.

Source: Archive of Our Own, Tags FAQ (Organization for Transformative Works) -- <https://archiveofourown.org/faq/tags>

## Declare your position on the canon spectrum in the header (compliant, compatible, divergent-at-point-X, or AU)

Fanlore's Canon Compliant entry: canon compliance means 'the fan writer made an effort to at least not contradict known canon details, plot developments, character back story' -- and 'fic headers often contain information about what the fic is compliant with (e.g. diverges after season 2, compliant with movie canon, etc.)'. The community distinguishes canon compliant (fits canon), canon compatible (not canon but contradicts nothing), and canon divergence (branches at a stated point). Actionable rule: every story states its compliance grade and, if divergent, the exact divergence point in its metadata.

Source: Fanlore wiki, 'Canon Compliant' entry (Organization for Transformative Works) -- <https://fanlore.org/wiki/Canon_Compliant>

## The AU recognizability test: transplanted characters must remain identifiable without their names

Fanlore's Alternate Universe entry records the community consensus: 'It's generally agreed that the most effective AUs are those in which -- even in the most radically changed circumstances -- the transplanted characters are clearly recognizable in relation to their canonical counterparts.' AU is a spectrum from a single-fact tweak (the Death Star is not destroyed) to 'radical AUs' that 'border on origfic', at which point fans ask why not 'file off the serial numbers' and publish it as original fiction. Test each AU scene: if you swapped the names out, would a fan still identify the characters by behavior and voice? If not, either pull the characterization back toward canon or reclassify the work as original fiction.

Source: Fanlore wiki, 'Alternate Universe' entry (Organization for Transformative Works) -- <https://fanlore.org/wiki/Alternate_Universe>

## OOC means contradiction of canon actions, not unusual interpretation -- and intentional OOC gets tagged

Fanlore's Out of Character entry gives a usable in-character test: 'Generally, only actions which directly contradict actions the character has taken in canon can be confidently called OOC', because interpretation is subjective. The second failure mode is characters 'so weakly defined that they could be replaced with anyone.' A quoted fan comment states the craft bar bluntly: 'PART OF GOOD WRITING IS GETTING THE CHARACTERS DOWN... why even call them Bodie and Doyle?' Practical loop: for each canon character, keep a voice sheet of canon actions and speech patterns; flag any scene that contradicts a canon action; if the OOC is deliberate, tag the work OOC (authors do this on AO3).

Source: Fanlore wiki, 'Out of Character' entry (Organization for Transformative Works) -- <https://fanlore.org/wiki/Out_of_Character>

## Judge OCs by trait clusters, not single traits (the litmus-test method), and use scores as a smell, not a gate

Mary Sue litmus tests (first one by Melissa 'Merlin Missy' Wilson, 1997, for Gargoyles fandom) are scored checklists: self-insert resemblance, wish-fulfillment, rare abilities, unearned relationships to canon characters, universal adoration, tragic backstory, dramatic death. Fanlore quotes the key methodological point: 'there are so many criteria that need to work together to make a Mary Sue' -- no single trait qualifies, and even a 'teenage girl discovers she's really omnipotent' can pass the test if the cast stays in character and no one inexplicably falls in love with her. The strongest concrete signals from the quoted categorization: everyone likes the OC on the spot, canon characters warp out of character around them, and the OC accomplishes what canon heroes could not.

Source: Fanlore wiki, 'Mary Sue Litmus Test' entry, citing Melissa Wilson's 1997 original test (Organization for Transformative Works) -- <https://fanlore.org/wiki/Mary_Sue_Litmus_Test>

## Stay non-commercial and transformative: that is the legal footing of the entire genre

The OTW (which runs AO3) states that transformative works add 'something new, with a further purpose or different character, altering the source with new expression, meaning or message', citing Campbell v. Acuff-Rose. Their stated view of what strengthens fair use: works that are non-commercial, transformative, limited in scope, and not substituting for the original -- 'None of these factors is absolutely necessary for fair use, but they all help.' They support the fannish gift economy and are 'against the commercialization of fanworks without their authors' consent.' For the sagas project: keep fan-shelf works free, never behind payment, and make each work add new meaning rather than reproduce source text.

Source: Organization for Transformative Works, FAQ -- <https://www.transformativeworks.org/faq/>

## Know the four statutory fair use factors and write to the favorable side of each

The US Copyright Office lists the 17 U.S.C. 107 factors: (1) purpose and character of the use, including whether commercial or nonprofit ('transformative' uses that add new value are favored); (2) nature of the copyrighted work (using highly creative works like novels weighs against fair use, so transformation matters more); (3) amount and substantiality used (do not reproduce the 'heart' of the source -- write new scenes, not retellings); (4) effect on the potential market (a fanwork must not substitute for buying the original). The page stresses determinations are fact-specific with no guaranteed formula.

Source: US Copyright Office, Fair Use Index / More Information on Fair Use -- <https://www.copyright.gov/fair-use/>

## Disclaimers are a good-faith community gesture, not legal protection -- keep them brief, informative, non-judgmental

Fanlore's Disclaimer entry: a disclaimer states the fan does not claim to own the copyrighted characters, typically also that 'they are making no profit from their fanwork'. Crucially, 'a disclaimer would probably not do much to deter a company... disclaimers are generally seen as a gesture of good faith from fans', and 'The Archive of Our Own does not offer a standard field for disclaimers, as their stance is that not-for-profit fanworks are Fair Use.' The quoted 1998 reader advice is the craft rule: 'just make your disclaimer and author's notes as brief, informative, and non-judgmental as possible. Let your work stand on its own merit' -- never apologize for the story, never say it 'sucks', never write more disclaimer than story.

Source: Fanlore wiki, 'Disclaimer' entry (Organization for Transformative Works) -- <https://fanlore.org/wiki/Disclaimer>

## Audit for fanon contamination: verify every 'fact' against actual canon before writing it as canon

Fanlore defines fanon as 'any element that is widely accepted among fans, but has little or no basis in canon' -- often 'something in a fanfic story that gets picked up on and repeated by other writers until it's so common that newbies might think it's a canonical fact.' Harry Potter's Fanon Draco / 'LeatherPants!Draco' is the canonical cautionary tale: Cassandra Claire's Draco Trilogy joke ('It was a joke at the time... everyone has assumed that somehow these leather pants are supposed to be serious') became a fandom-wide default characterization of a character who in canon is 'a low-class villain'. Rule for wizarding-world writing: for each character trait or world detail, cite the book/chapter it comes from; if you cannot, it is fanon or headcanon -- use it knowingly and tag it, or cut it.

Source: Fanlore wiki, 'Fanon' and 'Fanon Draco' entries (Organization for Transformative Works) -- <https://fanlore.org/wiki/Fanon_Draco>

## Anchor your story to a dated timeline and reconcile canon contradictions explicitly, the Lexicon way

The Harry Potter Lexicon's timeline work shows the discipline: dates are established by cross-referencing books, and revised when later canon lands ('I've been analyzing the timeline and I've made a few discoveries and revisions' after Half-Blood Prince contradicted earlier inferences); even official materials conflict (the Goblet of Fire film tombstone contradicts book dating). Actionable: before writing, build a dated event sheet for the span your story covers from a reference like the Lexicon; when canon sources conflict, pick one anchor (books over films is the fan norm), state that choice in your author's notes, and stay consistent with it across the serial.

Source: Steve Vander Ark et al., The Harry Potter Lexicon timeline essays -- <https://www.hp-lexicon.org/timeline/>

## Reread the source before diverging: canon facts beat plot convenience

StoryFlint's fan fiction guide condenses the pre-writing step: research the source thoroughly to understand how characters were originally portrayed, and 'Prioritize staying true to facts over convenience for your story, as this will help maintain a cohesive and believable universe for readers.' For OCs it prescribes the integration kit -- backstory, flaws, and a goal/motivation, with 'specific characteristics and attributes that separate them from the original cast' -- so the OC serves the story instead of overwriting the canon cast. It also repeats the credit norm: name the source material you are transforming.

Source: StoryFlint, 'Crafting the Perfect Fan Fiction: The Writer's Guide' -- <https://www.storyflint.com/blog/writing-fan-fiction>

## Caveats

Mary Sue litmus tests are genuinely contested: Fanlore documents that they fell out of favor from the mid-2000s because fans argued they 'fostered misogyny... shamed female fans, and made many fans unwilling to write original characters'; even creator Melissa Wilson later wished the test had not been used as it was. Treat the cluster-scoring method as a private revision heuristic, never a public gate. The famous 'Mary Sue' term itself predates the tests (Paula Smith, 1973 Star Trek zine parody -- not independently verified live this session). Fair use is fact-specific US doctrine with no bright-line formula (per copyright.gov), and the OTW is an advocacy organization, not a neutral authority; no court has squarely ruled on noncommercial fanfiction, and Warner Bros./J.K. Rowling's tolerance of HP fanworks is a policy that could change, not a right. Fanlore is a fan-edited wiki (near-primary for documenting conventions, but individual claims carry 'citation needed' flags in places).
