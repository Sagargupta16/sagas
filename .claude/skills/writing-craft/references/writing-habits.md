# Writing habits and blocks

Craft rules distilled from live-fetched sources and adversarially source-verified on 2026-07-11. Every attribution below survived a fact-check pass (refuted or corrected claims were fixed before inclusion). Apply as guidance, not dogma: when a rule fights the story, the story wins.

## Set a fixed daily quota and treat it as a job

King's stated practice is "I like to get ten pages a day, which amounts to 2,000 words," paired with his line "Amateurs sit and wait for inspiration, the rest of us just get up and go to work." The quota is output-based, not time-based: the session ends when the words exist, not when the clock says so. For an AI-assisted serial project, translate this to a per-chapter or per-session word target that gets hit before any polishing happens. King also caps drafting: a first draft "should take no more than three months, the length of a season," which argues for finishing serial arcs on a deadline rather than gardening them indefinitely.

Source: Stephen King, On Writing: A Memoir of the Craft (2000) -- <https://www.goodreads.com/work/quotes/150292-on-writing-a-memoir-of-the-craft>

## Timebox against a watch: 250 words every 15 minutes

Trollope's Autobiography (primary source, verified in the Gutenberg full text): "It was my practice to be at my table every morning at 5.30 A.M... to write with my watch before me, and to require from myself 250 words every quarter of an hour. I have found that the 250 words have been forthcoming as regularly as my watch went." He held that "three hours a day will produce as much as a man ought to write," yielding "over ten pages of an ordinary novel volume a day." The actionable rule: a short, fixed, clock-enforced session with a micro-quota removes the decision of whether to write, and small timed units (25o words/15 min is close to a Pomodoro) make progress measurable.

Source: Anthony Trollope, An Autobiography (1883), ch. XV -- <https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/5978/pg5978.txt>

## A small quota you never miss beats a big one you sometimes hit

Graham Greene wrote approximately 500 words a day in a small black leather notebook, then stopped; his editor Michael Korda described it as "Graham's daily penance -- once he finished he put the notebook away for the rest of the day." 500 words/day is tiny but compounds to a novel a year, and the hard stop prevents burnout and preserves appetite for tomorrow. For a serial, this argues for sizing the daily target to something sustainable on the worst day, not the best day.

Source: Graham Greene, as described by editor Michael Korda (cited on Wikipedia's Greene biography) -- <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graham_Greene>

## Stop mid-flow, while you still know what happens next

Hemingway wrote from first light until noon and advised stopping "when you know what is going to happen next" so the next session starts with momentum instead of a blank page. This is the classic anti-block device: never end a session at a natural stopping point; end it one sentence into the next scene. In serialized writing this applies per chapter: draft the first lines of chapter N+1 before closing the session on chapter N.

Source: Ernest Hemingway, on his daily routine (via The Marginalian's roundup of writers' routines) -- <https://www.themarginalian.org/2012/11/20/daily-routines-writers/>

## Open each session by rereading yesterday's pages to catch the tone

Trollope began every three-hour block "by reading the work of the day before, an operation which would take me half an hour, and which consisted chiefly in weighing with my ear the sound of the words." He explicitly recommends it to beginners: rereading the last stretch just before writing lets the writer "catch the tone and spirit of what he is then saying, and will avoid the fault of seeming to be unlike himself." For serials written across many sessions (or with an AI assistant), this is the voice-consistency mechanism: reload the previous chapter before drafting the next.

Source: Anthony Trollope, An Autobiography (1883), ch. XV -- <https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/5978/pg5978.txt>

## Write through the block badly, then re-attempt with fresh eyes

Sanderson says his block is not idea shortage but "a few chapters in and the story's not flowing." His fix is mechanical: write the problem chapter anyway, poorly, set it aside, and try again the next day; he reports this "fixes the problem almost every single time" and that on Firefight he only had to redo one chapter this way. The rule: a bad version of the scene is a diagnostic tool; never stop producing words to wait for the scene to feel right.

Source: Brandon Sanderson, official FAQ, "What do you do when you have writer's block?" (2018) -- <https://faq.brandonsanderson.com/knowledge-base/what-do-you-do-when-you-have-writers-block/>

## Put one word after another, and finish what you're writing

Gaiman's rules 2 and 3 (from his Guardian 'Ten rules for writing fiction' contribution): "Put one word after another. Find the right word, put it down" and "Finish what you're writing. Whatever you have to do to finish it, finish it." On revision he warns that perfection "is like chasing the horizon" -- "sooner or later... you will have to let it go and move on." For a serial library, finishing is the differentiator: an abandoned serial is worth less than a finished mediocre one, so shipping the chapter outranks perfecting it.

Source: Neil Gaiman, '8 Rules of Writing' (originally The Guardian, 2010; via The Marginalian) -- <https://www.themarginalian.org/2012/09/28/neil-gaiman-8-rules-of-writing/>

## Grant explicit permission for a shitty first draft

Lamott's core claims, verified verbatim: "Perfectionism is the voice of the oppressor, the enemy of the people. It will keep you cramped and insane your whole life, and it is the main obstacle between you and a shitty first draft" and "Almost all good writing begins with terrible first efforts. You need to start somewhere." Her method is the 'child's draft': let the first pass be unguarded and private, because nobody sees it. Operationally: separate the drafting pass from the judging pass, and never edit sentence-by-sentence while drafting. Scope antidote: "Bird by bird, buddy. Just take it bird by bird" -- break the book into the next small piece only.

Source: Anne Lamott, Bird by Bird: Some Instructions on Writing and Life (1994) -- <https://www.goodreads.com/work/quotes/841198-bird-by-bird-some-instructions-on-writing-and-life>

## Don't break the chain: track the streak, not the quality

The Seinfeld method as told by comedian Brad Isaac: put a big wall calendar up, mark a red X for every day you write, and "after a few days you'll have a chain... Your only job is to not break the chain." James Clear's analysis of why it works: "it helps to take the focus off of each individual performance and puts the emphasis on the process instead" -- consistency is decoupled from motivation and from how good any single day's output is. For a GitHub-based serial this maps directly onto the commit streak: a green square per day of prose is the chain.

Source: Jerry Seinfeld (as recounted by Brad Isaac), analyzed by James Clear, 'The Seinfeld Strategy' -- <https://jamesclear.com/stop-procrastinating-seinfeld-strategy>

## "Write drunk, edit sober" is misattributed folklore -- do not treat it as craft advice

Quote Investigator found no evidence Hemingway ever said it; the earliest match is Peter De Vries's 1964 novel Reuben, Reuben, where the Dylan Thomas-modeled character Gowan McGland says: "Sometimes I write drunk and revise sober... and sometimes I write sober and revise drunk." Note it is dialogue describing a character's inconsistent habits, not prescriptive advice, and Hemingway himself denied drinking while writing (1961 interview, published in Writer's Digest 1964). The legitimate kernel worth keeping: draft in a loose, uninhibited mode and revise in a critical, detached mode -- the same two-pass separation King and Lamott formalize.

Source: Peter De Vries, Reuben, Reuben (1964); attribution debunked by Quote Investigator (2016) -- <https://quoteinvestigator.com/2016/09/21/write-drunk/>

## Sprint with quantity-over-quality deadlines to force a finished draft

NaNoWriMo's design: 50,000 words in November, averaging 1,667 words a day, where "writers are instructed to prioritize speed and quantity over quality, with the 50,000 word count being the primary goal," using "the deadline as an incentive to get the story going." The model demonstrably produces real books: since 2006, nearly 400 NaNoWriMo drafts were traditionally published and 200+ small-press or self-published. The transferable rule for serials: a hard external deadline plus a public word-count target converts perfectionism into throughput; editing is a separate, later phase ("editing in December").

Source: National Novel Writing Month (NaNoWriMo) methodology, per Wikipedia's sourced overview -- <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Novel_Writing_Month>

## Start the next book the day you finish the last (no victory-lap gap)

Trollope's shoemaker analogy, verified in the primary text: "A shoemaker when he has finished one pair of shoes does not sit down and contemplate his work in idle satisfaction... I had now quite accustomed myself to begin a second pair as soon as the first was out of my hands." This pipeline habit let him keep "one or two or even three unpublished novels in my desk beside me" -- i.e., a publication buffer. He also dismisses waiting for inspiration outright: "I was once told that the surest aid to the writing of a book was a piece of cobbler's wax on my chair. I certainly believe in the cobbler's wax much more than the inspiration." For a serial library this is the buffer rule: keep finished chapters banked ahead of the release schedule, and roll from one book straight into the next.

Source: Anthony Trollope, An Autobiography (1883), chs. VII and XVII -- <https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/5978/pg5978.txt>

## Caveats

1. Seinfeld chain: the story is secondhand via Brad Isaac (original Lifehacker post was unreachable; James Clear's retelling was fetched instead). Seinfeld has reportedly downplayed inventing the method in a 2014 Reddit AMA, but I could not fetch that AMA live, so treat the attribution as "Seinfeld-via-Isaac" (unverified at origin), while the technique itself is well documented. 2) "Write drunk, edit sober" is definitively NOT Hemingway per Quote Investigator; the De Vries line is character dialogue, descriptive not prescriptive, and Hemingway denied drinking while writing. 3) Neil Gaiman's famous NaNoWriMo pep talk ("you write on the good days and you write on the lousy days") could not be fetched -- nanowrimo.org returns an empty page and archive mirrors were blocked -- so it is omitted; his 8 rules were verified via The Marginalian's reproduction of the 2010 Guardian piece instead. 4) King's 2,000-words/10-pages figure and door-closed line were verified via the Goodreads quote page for On Writing, not the publisher's text (MasterClass and Farnam Street pages 403'd/404'd); the quotes match the book as I know it but the hosting source is tertiary. 5) Greene's 500 words/day rests on editor Michael Korda's recollection as cited on Wikipedia; Greene's own Paris Review interview was unreachable. 6) NaNoWriMo the organization announced closure in 2025 (interim board statement, March 2025) -- the method survives independent of the org; this closure detail is from training memory, not verified live in this session. 7) Trollope quotes are from the Project Gutenberg full text downloaded and grepped this session -- these are primary-source verified.
