# Description and sensory grounding

Craft rules distilled from live-fetched sources and adversarially source-verified on 2026-07-11. Every attribution below survived a fact-check pass (refuted or corrected claims were fixed before inclusion). Apply as guidance, not dogma: when a rule fights the story, the story wins.

## Never open in a white room -- anchor the scene in concrete setting details before anything else happens

The Turkey City Lexicon defines White Room Syndrome as 'a clear and common sign of the failure of the writer's imagination, most often seen at the beginning of a story, before the setting, background, or characters have gelled', with the classic symptom 'She awoke in a white room.' The fix is mechanical: before dialogue or introspection starts, plant at least two or three specific, sensory, setting-bound details so the reader has a stage. Related lexicon entries sharpen the rule: avoid 'rear-view mirror description' (describing a thing only after it has figured in the action; describe it before it is used) and 'false interiorization' (using blindfolds, darkness, or illness as an excuse not to describe surroundings).

Source: Turkey City Lexicon, SF workshop lexicon edited by Lewis Shiner and Bruce Sterling (mirrored on FritzWiki) -- <https://critters.org/turkeycity.html>

## Build the Pyramid of Abstraction: concrete sensory description at the base, abstract ideas only on top of it

Sanderson teaches that 'descriptions form the base of a pyramid, grounding the reader in the story' with abstract concepts at the top: use concrete language to establish setting before introducing abstract ideas, because concrete descriptions 'earn the right' to use abstraction (BYU Lecture 6, ~00:13:58 in these notes). Practical application per scene: ground first (place, bodies, objects, light), then let theme, emotion, or exposition ride on that base; when readers feel lost during conceptual passages, the fix is usually to pull back down the pyramid. He also cautions that 'show versus tell is not something to hold up as absolute gold standard' because showing costs words -- the pyramid is a rationing tool, not a mandate to show everything.

Source: Brandon Sanderson, BYU creative writing lectures (2020), community lecture notes -- <https://gist.github.com/jake9696/86f1f9d65af275d77435f6b33c78d64b>

## Chekhov's detail-selection principle: a few small, grouped, specific details that assemble into a picture beat any panoramic description

What Chekhov actually wrote to his brother Alexander in May 1886: 'In descriptions of Nature one must seize on small details, grouping them so that when the reader closes his eyes he gets a picture. For instance, you'll have a moonlit night if you write that on the mill dam a piece of glass from a broken bottle glittered like a bright little star, and that the black shadow of a dog or a wolf rolled past like a ball.' As a rule: to evoke a large phenomenon (moonlight, a battle, a city), pick one or two tiny concrete images that imply it, rather than describing the whole. The famous 'Don't tell me the moon is shining; show me the glint of light on broken glass' is an apocryphal condensation of this letter, per Quote Investigator.

Source: Anton Chekhov, letter to Alexander Chekhov (May 1886), authenticity traced by Quote Investigator -- <https://quoteinvestigator.com/2013/07/30/moon-glint/>

## Filter every description through the POV character's occupation, mood, and attitude -- 'the baker will notice the butter'

In Writing Excuses 8.31 the hosts state the double-duty rule directly: 'you use description to establish your scenic location... use your POV character to also... tell us something about the character' (Kowal), and 'you can use that to demonstrate so much about who a character is based on what they notice and how they notice it' (Wells). Kowal's memorable formula: 'the baker will notice the butter on the counter, the painter will notice the crooked painting.' Operationally: before describing a room, ask what THIS character would notice first, what they would name it, and what their word choice reveals; a description that any character could have produced is a wasted sentence.

Source: Brandon Sanderson, Mary Robinette Kowal, Dan Wells, Howard Tayler, Writing Excuses 8.31 'Combining Dialogue, Blocking, and Description' (2013) -- <https://writingexcuses.com/writing-excuses-8-31-combining-dialogue-blocking-and-description/>

## Use about three senses per scene, not all five, and describe the small things so readers imagine the large ones

In Writing Excuses 12.7 Kowal warns against sensory checklisting: 'nobody actually looks at something and goes, how does everything look, sound... I'm going to lick this vase' -- her guideline is that three senses build a scene effectively while forcing all five overdoes it. The episode adds Dan Abnett's principle, quoted by Sanderson: 'describe the small things and let them imagine the large things.' Concretely: pick the two or three senses this moment would actually engage (favor the underused ones -- smell, texture, temperature -- over sight), render them in small specific details, and let the reader's imagination construct the panorama.

Source: Wesley Chu (three senses) and Dan Wells as quoted by Brandon Sanderson (small/large details), Writing Excuses 12.7 (2017) -- <https://writingexcuses.com/12-7-description-through-the-third-person-lens/>

## Establish geography before you need it: no object or exit may appear at the moment it becomes useful

Writing Excuses 8.18 on blocking: introduce the terrain before the action requires it (Kowal cites Jackie Chan films, which show the props and spatial layout before the fight uses them), and Sanderson warns that characters must not 'mysteriously appear with objects' -- establish an item's presence before a character uses it. Unclear blocking pulls readers out of immersion, so track where every participant is standing and re-anchor after movement. For chaotic fights, Tayler recommends 'fog of war' (rendering the POV character's confusion honestly) over disorienting blow-by-blow, and Sanderson drafts the emotional beats of a fight first, adding spatial choreography afterward.

Source: Brandon Sanderson, Mary Robinette Kowal, Dan Wells, Howard Tayler, Writing Excuses 8.18 'Blocking' (2013) -- <https://writingexcuses.com/writing-excuses-8-18-blocking/>

## Make blocking purposeful: beats frequent at dialogue openings, then roughly every 4-5 lines mid-dialogue

Two paired rules from Writing Excuses. From 8.18: every character movement should convey emotion or motivation ('action is reaction' -- Kowal); physical beats replace repetitive said-tags and remind readers the scene is three-dimensional, and detailed physical description can mark the passage of time instead of stating 'nobody spoke for a minute' (Tayler). From 8.31: Sanderson recommends placing a blocking/description beat about every four or five lines during intense dialogue, increasing beat frequency during calmer moments to re-anchor readers in the space. This gives an AI-checkable heuristic: long dialogue runs with zero physical anchoring are a defect.

NOTE: corrected per source: Sanderson uses a beat in nearly every line at the START of a dialogue to ground the scene, then sparser beats every four or five lines in the MIDDLE. The tense-vs-calm framing in earlier summaries is not in the episode.

Source: Brandon Sanderson, Writing Excuses 8.31 (2013) -- <https://writingexcuses.com/writing-excuses-8-18-blocking/>

## Every description must do more than one job, and doing this makes prose leaner, not more purple

Writing Excuses 6.11's test for any descriptive passage: what does this say about the character observing it, and how does it advance the story? Word choice carries the second job: 'cluttered' versus cataloging 'a plate left on the counter from yesterday's breakfast' reveals how much the observer cares and whether it is their home. Sanderson's corollary kills the purple-prose worry: 'if you're doing what this podcast is saying, it should make your prose less purple' -- multi-purpose description means fewer words, not more. Cut any description that only paints scenery ('gorilla in the phone booth' details that distract from momentum fail the test).

Source: Brandon Sanderson, Mary Robinette Kowal, Dan Wells, Howard Tayler, Writing Excuses 6.11 'Making Your Descriptions Do More Than One Thing' (2011) -- <https://writingexcuses.com/writing-excuses-6-11-making-your-descriptions-do-more-than-one-thing/>

## Crowd and leap: crowd the scene with vivid detail where it counts, then leap over everything else -- 'what you leap over is what you leave out'

Le Guin's Chapter 10 of Steering the Craft names the two moves of scene-level pacing: crowding (filling the moment with rich sensory detail) and leaping (skipping what does not count). Her drafting advice: 'go ahead and crowd in the first draft -- tell it all, blab, babble, put everything in', then in revision cut what 'merely pads or repeats or slows or impedes your story': 'Decide what counts, what tells, and cut and recombine till what's left is what counts.' The deeper principle: 'There's got to be white space around the word, silence around the voice' -- strategic omission is what lets readers co-create the scene, and a story must always leave out far more than it includes. For an assistant, this converts to: dense sensory rendering at emotionally load-bearing moments, summary or outright skips between them, never uniform description density.

Source: Ursula K. Le Guin, Steering the Craft (1998/2015), Chapter 10 'Crowding and Leaping', quoted in Margin Chronicles' close reading -- <https://marginchronicles.com/site/articles/leaping-over-dialogue.html>

## Ration adjectives and adverbs: when tempted to modify, choose a stronger noun or verb instead

Le Guin's Chapter 5 of Steering the Craft treats adjectives and adverbs as structurally extraneous: overuse weakens their power, interrupts flow, and 'the unexpected and thought provoking use of a descriptor can take the reader away from the moment.' Her exercise ('Chastity') is to write a descriptive passage using no adjectives or adverbs at all, forcing the description into concrete nouns and active verbs; she notes the goal is not permanently 'chaste' prose but training the reflex. Rule form: in descriptive passages, flag stacked modifiers ('softly glowing pale blue light') and rewrite around one precise noun/verb; adverbs on dialogue tags and -ly adverbs generally are first cuts.

Source: Ursula K. Le Guin, Steering the Craft, Chapter 5 'Adjectives and Adverbs', via a chapter-by-chapter reading at Would That I Might -- <https://wouldthatimight.blogspot.com/2018/07/steering-craft-chapter-five-adjectives.html>

## Control psychic distance like a camera: zoom deliberately along Gardner's five-step ladder and never cut jerkily between steps

Gardner's ladder runs from remote ('It was winter of the year 1853. A large man stepped out of a doorway.') through neutral report, personalized statement ('Henry hated snowstorms'), free indirect style ('God how he hated these damn snowstorms'), to full immersion ('Snow. Under your collar, down inside your shoes, freezing and plugging up your miserable soul'). Emma Darwin's practical rules: open a scene wider (orienting the reader in space and time) and move closer as stakes rise; at close distance the character's own vocabulary must reshape the narration, so a snow-loving character and a snow-hating one describe the same weather differently. The named failure modes are prolonged distance (detached, monotonous), prolonged extreme closeness (claustrophobic), and unmotivated jumps between rungs that disorient readers. This is the mechanism behind 'description filtered through POV': distance level dictates whose words describe the room.

Source: John Gardner, The Art of Fiction (1983), explicated by Emma Darwin, 'Psychic Distance: What It Is and How To Use It' (This Itch of Writing, now on Substack) -- <https://emmadarwin.substack.com/p/psychic-distance-what-it-is-and-how>

## Treat setting as a character: if the story could happen anywhere, the setting is not working hard enough

LaMarca's test: ask whether the story could occur elsewhere -- if not, the setting functions as a character and should get character-grade treatment. Six concrete jobs to assign it per scene or arc: set mood/atmosphere, generate conflict (McCarthy's The Road, where 'the bleak and desolate landscape is a constant threat'), foreshadow (weather and environment signaling coming events), transform alongside the protagonist (an overgrown garden mirroring decline), shape narrative voice (rural introspective prose vs urban terse prose), and carry symbolism. For serialized fantasy this means recurring locations should change on the page as the story's emotional state changes, not remain static backdrops.

Source: Elle LaMarca, 'Setting as Character in Fiction Writing', Writers.com (2024-06-18) -- <https://writers.com/feature/setting-as-character-in-fiction-writing>

## Caveats

The famous Chekhov line "Don't tell me the moon is shining; show me the glint of light on broken glass" is apocryphal -- Quote Investigator traces it to an anonymous condensation of his May 1886 letter to his brother; craft sites (including Reedsy's show-don't-tell guide, fetched) still repeat it as a direct quote. Relatedly, "show, don't tell" itself is contested as an absolute: Sanderson's lecture notes call it "not... an absolute gold standard" because showing costs words, and Reedsy's guide concedes telling is correct for quick transitions and post-reveal clarity. Source-quality notes: Le Guin's Steering the Craft and Gardner's The Art of Fiction are books without free full text, so their rules are cited via close-reading blog posts and Emma Darwin's essay (near-primary but paraphrase-dependent); Sanderson's pyramid-of-abstraction comes from community lecture notes (GitHub gist) plus his own words on Writing Excuses 8.31, not an official transcript. The SFWA-hosted Turkey City Lexicon returned HTTP 403, so the FritzWiki mirror was used; the lexicon is collaboratively authored (Shiner/Sterling edited), so entry-level attribution is collective. The Writing Excuses "every 4-5 lines" beat rhythm and "three senses not five" are host heuristics from podcast discussion, not tested doctrine -- treat as defaults, not hard limits.
