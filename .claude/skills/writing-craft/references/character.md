# Character craft

Craft rules distilled from live-fetched sources and adversarially source-verified on 2026-07-11. Every attribution below survived a fact-check pass (refuted or corrected claims were fixed before inclusion). Apply as guidance, not dogma: when a rule fights the story, the story wins.

## Build the arc on the Lie the Character Believes

Every change arc starts from a specific false belief about the world ("The Change Arc is all about the Lie Your Character Believes"). The character's flawed choices are symptoms of the Lie; the arc is the journey from Lie to a liberating Truth. Actionable: write the Lie down as one sentence before drafting, and make sure the arc's beats land on the plot's structural turning points (First Plot Point, Midpoint, Third Plot Point, Climax) so change feels earned rather than announced.

Source: K.M. Weiland, Creating Character Arcs (summarized on her site Helping Writers Become Authors) -- <https://helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/write-character-arcs/>

## Keep Want and Need distinct, and derive each from Lie/Truth

The Want is the character's conscious goal, a false fix flowing from the Lie; the Need is the Truth they must internalize. Plot momentum comes from pursuing the Want; theme comes from discovering the Need. Rules: make the Want visible and actively pursued early, keep Want and Need from collapsing into one thing, delay full recognition of the Need until near the climax, and widen the gap between them for a stronger arc ("the bigger the ghost, the bigger the Lie, the bigger the arc").

Source: K.M. Weiland, 'Creating Stunning Character Arcs, Pt. 3: The Thing Your Character Wants vs. The Thing Your Character Needs' -- <https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/character-arcs-3/>

## Pick one of three arc shapes per character: positive, flat, or negative

Positive arc: Lie to Truth transformation (Bilbo, Scrooge). Flat arc: the character already holds the Truth and changes the world around them instead (Atticus Finch); they are tested, not transformed. Negative arc: the character rejects the Truth and falls, in three variants Weiland names Disillusionment, Fall, and Corruption (Gatsby, Walter White). Actionable: decide the arc shape per major character before outlining a book, since flat-arc protagonists need supporting characters who do the changing.

Source: K.M. Weiland's typology, echoed in Reedsy's character-arc guide -- <https://reedsy.com/blog/character-arc/>

## Set Sanderson's three sliders; do not max all three

Rate each character 1-10 on proactivity, relatability (likability), and capability. Most compelling characters excel at one, are moderate on another, and grow in the third over the story; moving a slider IS a visible arc, which also lets flat-arc characters feel dynamic without changing their convictions (Sherlock: capability 9, likability 3, and the story raises his likability). Actionable: write the three numbers into the character sheet at story start and story end.

Source: Brandon Sanderson, BYU Writing Lecture #5 (2025), Three Pillars of Compelling Characters; scale examples via September C. Fawkes -- <https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/creating-proactive-relatable-and-capable-characters-brandon-sandersons-writing-lecture-5-2025>

## Proactivity first: show small acts of agency in chapter one

Sanderson calls proactivity "the single most important element that makes a character readable," and notes villains are often more proactive than heroes. If the plot has not started yet or the character is trapped, still show them solving small problems (Mulan inventing a chicken-feeding rig), planning, and attempting things even when they fail. He also estimates roughly 60% of character problems trace to unclear motivation, so state on the page what the character wants and why before asking readers to follow them.

Source: Brandon Sanderson, BYU Writing Lecture #5 (2025) -- <https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/creating-proactive-relatable-and-capable-characters-brandon-sandersons-writing-lecture-5-2025>

## Write moment-to-moment cause and effect as Motivation-Reaction Units

Alternate an external, objective Motivation ("present it as a videocamera would capture it") with the POV character's Reaction. The Reaction must follow physiological order: feeling first, reflex second, rational action and speech last; "If there is a Feeling, it must come first." Skipping parts is fine, reordering them is not, because reversed order (speaking before flinching) breaks the reader's illusion of lived experience.

Source: Dwight V. Swain, Techniques of the Selling Writer, as taught by Randy Ingermanson in Writing the Perfect Scene -- <https://advancedfictionwriting.com/articles/writing-the-perfect-scene/>

## Structure chapters as Scene (goal-conflict-disaster) then Sequel (reaction-dilemma-decision)

A Scene gives the character a concrete goal, throws conflict at it, and ends in disaster; a Sequel processes that disaster emotionally, weighs a dilemma, and ends in a decision that becomes the next Scene's goal. This is character craft as much as plot craft: the Sequel is where interiority, want/need tension, and voice live. For serialized web fiction the disaster-then-decision loop doubles as a natural chapter-ending hook engine.

Source: Dwight V. Swain via Randy Ingermanson, Writing the Perfect Scene -- <https://advancedfictionwriting.com/articles/writing-the-perfect-scene/>

## Give each character a voice toolkit: tics, metaphor families, rhythm; never lean on one tool

Weiland's concrete levers: dialogue tics (favored words, sentence length, volume), personalized slang and swears, metaphor families drawn from "their job, their home region, or their psychological state," sparing catchphrases, and distinct rhythms (fragments vs. run-ons vs. formal grammar). Ground the choices in nine background factors (personality, emotional stance, conflict style, age, education, region, dialect, profession). Overusing any single device turns "cartoonish and self-indulgent," so distribute two or three per character.

Source: K.M. Weiland, Helping Writers Become Authors, on character voice -- <https://helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/character-voice/>

## The dialogue-swap test, plus page-time budgets, for ensemble casts

"If a reader cannot tell your characters apart in dialogue, your ensemble has failed": differentiate by vocabulary/rhythm, worldview, and interior decision logic (emotional vs. calculated vs. deferential). Operational guardrails for multi-POV serials: track chapters per character in a spreadsheet, keep each lead between roughly 15% and 35% of page time, give every POV its own story engine (a want, an obstacle, a ticking clock), introduce the full cast in the first quarter, and plan at least three full-cast convergence scenes (early, midpoint, climax). A Song of Ice and Fire is the model: each POV is the protagonist of their own drama.

NOTE: the source's actual test is a dialogue-SWAP test (if you can swap dialogue between two characters without the scene changing, their voices are not distinct enough), not a cover-the-names test. The page-time budget (no major character below 15% or above 35% of page time) IS supported.

Source: Chapter blog, How to Write an Ensemble Cast -- <https://blog.chapter.pub/how-to-write-an-ensemble-cast/>

## Antagonists get the protagonist treatment: human face, equal or greater capability

DiSilverio's core definition: the antagonist is "the person who acts to keep your protagonist from achieving his goals." Rules: never use an abstraction (war, greed) as the antagonist, personify it; give the villain backstory, regrets, and moments of kindness; make them at least a match for the hero (complementary strengths like intellect vs. strength work well); when the antagonist is a friend or family member, exploit that for tension; and use proxies for a hidden big bad so stakes escalate. She invokes "no one is the villain in his own story" and even suggests trying the antagonist as a viewpoint character.

Source: Laura DiSilverio, Writer's Digest, 6 Ways to Write Better Bad Guys -- <https://writersdigest.com/write-better-fiction/6-ways-to-write-better-bad-guys>

## Character sheets in three layers, but core wound and motivation outrank trivia

Reedsy structures profiles as outer layer (appearance, speech, mannerisms), middle layer (backstory, family, formative memories), and core layer (goals, motivations, fears, moral compass). Its editors warn that "creating dossiers can get in the way of writing the book" and that over-detailed profiles freeze characters who should "grow and evolve." Rule for AI-assisted serials: keep the sheet minimal at draft time (wound, want, need, voice notes), answer WHY they want what they want, and treat the sheet as a consistency reference, not a spec.

Source: Reedsy blog, character profile guide (multiple professional editors quoted) -- <https://reedsy.com/blog/character-profile/>

## Maintain a living story bible as the single source of truth for serialized characters

For anything serialized, record each character's physical appearance, habits and mannerisms, psychology, motivations, conflicts, relationships, and key life events, and update the bible as the draft evolves rather than only at the start: "a story bible prevents continuity errors by ensuring that every detail within your world is consistent and accurate." Consult it during drafting and revision both. Related process note from Sanderson: for multi-POV books he drafts each viewpoint straight through within a part ("I wrote Kaladin straight through and then Shallan straight through") to hold each voice steady, then interleaves; a per-character bible page makes that interleaving safe.

Source: Daniel Redbird, 'Creating a Story Bible for Fiction Writers', Behind the Draft -- <https://behindthedraft.com/how-to-create-a-story-bible/>

## Caveats

Attribution and contestation notes: (1) "No one is the villain in his own story" is a floating aphorism; DiSilverio uses it unattributed, and its likely ancestor is John Barth's "everyone is necessarily the hero of his own life story", so treat it as folk wisdom, not a quotable source. (2) Swain's strict feeling-reflex-rational-action MRU order is prescriptive and contested; many working editors treat MRUs as a revision diagnostic rather than an ironclad drafting law, and rigid application produces mechanical prose. (3) Sanderson's pillar names vary between tellings (likability vs relatability, competence vs capability); the 2025 official lecture writeup says "relatability" and "capability", while secondary summaries (Fawkes) say "likability" and "competence". His "60% of character problems stem from unclear motivation" is a lecture estimate, not research. (4) Chapter blog's numeric guardrails (15-35% page time, three reunion scenes, first-quarter cast introduction) are that blog's own heuristics, not established doctrine; the no-names dialogue test itself is widely repeated across craft sources. (5) Want-vs-need language predates Weiland (it traces through screenwriting theory, e.g. Vogler/McKee); Weiland's Lie/Truth framing is her specific contribution. (6) masterclass.com and www.writersdigest.com blocked or failed DNS during research; the Writer's Digest villain article was fetched via the non-www domain, and no MasterClass source could be verified live, so none is cited.
