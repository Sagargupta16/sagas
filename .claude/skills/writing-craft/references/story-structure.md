# Story structure and plotting

Craft rules distilled from live-fetched sources and adversarially source-verified on 2026-07-11. Every attribution below survived a fact-check pass (refuted or corrected claims were fixed before inclusion). Apply as guidance, not dogma: when a rule fights the story, the story wins.

## Three-act proportions: 25/50/25, chained by cause-and-effect

Divide the story into Act One setup (~25%), Act Two confrontation (~50%), Act Three resolution (~25% or less), with an inciting incident inside Act One, a midpoint stakes-raiser, and a plot point at each act break where the protagonist commits (Act 1->2) and turns proactive (Act 2->3). The operative rule for serials: 'every scene must lead into what happens next and not be a standalone episode' -- the story must be a chain of cause-and-effect beats, not disconnected events. For a serialized book, apply the proportions per volume/arc, not per chapter.

Source: Reedsy editorial team, 'Three-Act Structure' guide (structure popularized for modern screen/novel use by Syd Field's Screenplay, 1979) -- <https://reedsy.com/blog/guide/story-structure/three-act-structure/>

## Save the Cat: 15 beats pinned to percentages, with 'wiggle room' for novels

Blake Snyder's beat sheet fixes 15 beats to positions: Opening Image 1%, Theme Stated 5%, Catalyst 10%, Debate 10-20%, Break Into Two 20-30%, B Story ~22%, Fun and Games ('promise of the premise') 20-50%, Midpoint false victory 50%, Bad Guys Close In 50-75%, All Is Lost 75%, Dark Night of the Soul 75-80%, Break Into Three 80%, Finale 80-99%, Final Image mirroring the opening at 100%. Test each beat with the fill-in-the-blank check quoted from an editor in the guide: 'if I can't fill in the blank in one or two sentences -- I don't have a beat yet.' The guide stresses novelists have 'much more wiggle room' on placement than screenwriters; treat the sheet as a diagnostic, not a template.

Source: Blake Snyder, Save the Cat! (2005), as summarized with novelist adaptations by Reedsy -- <https://reedsy.com/blog/guide/story-structure/save-the-cat-beat-sheet/>

## Hero's Journey (Vogler's 12 stages): use only for single-protagonist transformation arcs

Christopher Vogler condensed Joseph Campbell's 17-stage monomyth into 12 stages (Ordinary World, Call, Refusal, Mentor, First Threshold, Tests/Allies/Enemies, Approach to the Inmost Cave, Ordeal, Reward, Road Back, Resurrection, Return with the Elixir). The source is explicit about fit: best for stories where a single protagonist's transformation is central; a poor fit for ensemble casts with multiple equally important characters -- which describes most long epic fantasy. Practical rule for the sagas project: apply the Journey to one POV character's book-length arc, not to the series or to multi-POV volumes.

Source: Christopher Vogler, The Writer's Journey (1992), adapting Joseph Campbell, The Hero with a Thousand Faces (1949); Reedsy guide -- <https://reedsy.com/blog/guide/story-structure/heros-journey/>

## Dan Harmon's Story Circle: 8 steps, one full circle per episode/chapter unit

Harmon's cycle: 1) character in a zone of comfort, 2) they want something, 3) enter an unfamiliar situation, 4) adapt, 5) get what they wanted, 6) pay a heavy price, 7) return to the familiar, 8) having changed. He built it by streamlining Campbell for practical TV writing, and its serialized advantage is structural: the character 'returns to the beginning' at step 7-8, resetting the board for the next installment while accumulating psychological change across episodes (the Rick and Morty pattern). For a web serial, run one circle per chapter or per short arc and let the 'changed' state carry forward -- it is the single framework in this set designed for repeated, episodic use.

Source: Dan Harmon (developed for Channel 101 and Community), as documented in Reedsy's Story Circle guide -- <https://reedsy.com/blog/guide/story-structure/dan-harmon-story-circle/>

## Sanderson's Promise: make tone, story, character, and structural promises in chapter one

Sanderson identifies four promise types made at a story's opening -- tone (humor/darkness via prose style), story (ultimate direction), character/conflict (who the protagonist is and what blocks them), and structural (genre conventions). His stated rule: 'The first chapter and first few pages are when people will decide whether or not they're going to put your book down.' For a serial this applies twice over: the first chapter promises the book, and each chapter's opening re-promises what that installment will deliver.

Source: Brandon Sanderson, 2025 BYU Guide to Plot, Lecture 2 (his own site) -- <https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/brandon-sandersons-2025-guide-to-plot-lecture-2>

## Sanderson's Progress: readers quit when there are no signposts of progress

Progress is the reader's sense that the narrative advances meaningfully -- via plot events, information reveals (mystery clues), relationship evolution, or internal character growth -- not merely time passing. Sanderson's diagnostic: 'A big reason that people drop off a book is that there weren't enough signposts of progress.' His actionable technique is to use chapter openings as signposts showing what is advancing. This is the highest-leverage rule for serialized fiction, where drop-off happens between installments: every chapter must visibly move at least one tracked thread.

Source: Brandon Sanderson, 2025 BYU Guide to Plot, Lecture 2 (his own site) -- <https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/brandon-sandersons-2025-guide-to-plot-lecture-2>

## Sanderson's Payoff: 'surprising but fulfilling answer to your promise', cycled per subplot

A payoff must keep the core promise while creating genuine doubt on the way -- the reader should believe resolution is possible, fear it won't come, and feel both surprise and inevitability when it lands. For multi-threaded epics, Sanderson scales the framework: each subplot runs its own promise-progress-payoff cycle that also feeds the 'Big P Plot'. Serial application: interleave threads so something pays off regularly (chapter-level and arc-level payoffs) while the series-level promise stays open.

Source: Brandon Sanderson, 2025 BYU Guide to Plot, Lecture 2 (his own site) -- <https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/brandon-sandersons-2025-guide-to-plot-lecture-2>

## Weiland's beat percentages: time major structural points to within 5%

K.M. Weiland pins nine beats: Hook 1%, Inciting Event 12%, First Plot Point 25% (a point of no return, protagonist 'violently thrust from their Normal World'), First Pinch Point 37%, Midpoint 50% (the swivel from reaction to action), Second Pinch Point 62%, Third Plot Point 75% (darkest moment, 'very often feature[s] death or the threat of death'), Climax 88%, Resolution 98%. Her explicit tolerance: aim 'to time the major structural points to within 5% of the optimal', with more slack in longer narratives. The Climactic Moment must answer the dramatic question first posed at the Inciting Event -- a checkable pairing an AI assistant can verify per book.

Source: K.M. Weiland, 'Story Structure' hub / Structuring Your Novel (helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com, her own site) -- <https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/story-structure/>

## Dan Wells' seven-point structure: plot backwards from the Resolution

The seven points are Hook, Plot Turn 1, Pinch 1, Midpoint (reactive to proactive), Pinch 2 ('all is lost'), Plot Turn 2, Resolution. Wells' core rule is to decide the Resolution first, then write a Hook that is its opposite state ('Wherever the protagonist ends up, you can have them start in the opposite position. This gives the story a promise of transformation'), then fill Midpoint, then the turns and pinches. This backwards, skeleton-first method is well suited to serials because you can lock the seven points per arc before drafting chapters, keeping a discovery-written serial from drifting.

Source: Dan Wells, 'Seven-Point Story Structure' lecture series (originally presented at LTUE/Writing Excuses), as documented by Reedsy -- <https://reedsy.com/blog/guide/story-structure/seven-point-story-structure/>

## Gardener vs architect: pick per project, but know your ending if you want engineered payoffs

George R.R. Martin's framing: architects plan the whole house with blueprints before writing; gardeners plant a seed and discover the story as it grows; Martin calls himself 'much more a gardener than an architect.' Sanderson, responding to the same framing on his FAQ, says 'I do outline quite extensively' but 'an outline has to be a living thing of its own', and he deliberately discovery-writes bounded zones (Stormlight interludes) inside an architected plot. His stated tradeoff is the actionable rule: outlining is better for foreshadowing and engineered endings ('I have to know where I'm going before I start'), discovery writing better for humor and voice. For an AI-assisted serial: architect the arc skeleton, garden inside the chapter.

Source: George R.R. Martin (widely-quoted architects/gardeners statement, via Goodreads) and Brandon Sanderson (his own FAQ) -- <https://faq.brandonsanderson.com/knowledge-base/are-you-an-outliner-or-a-discovery-writer/>

## Serial logistics (Wildbow): 12-16 chapter backlog before launch; consistency is king

Wildbow (Worm, ~1.7M words serialized) gives concrete operational rules: 'I really, really recommend writing yourself a backlog -- 12 to 16 chapters you've already got done before you start uploading', both to test your sustainable pace and to absorb life disruptions without missing updates. His three pillars are consistency, frequency, and quality -- consistency first, because readers build habits around predictable schedules and drift away from irregular ones. He also warns against launching without a buffer, expecting fast readership growth, and monetizing before proving consistency. Directly applicable to a GitHub-published serial: keep unpublished chapters in the repo ahead of the release cursor.

Source: Wildbow (John C. McCrae), 'Thoughts on Writing Serials', his own blog (2017) -- <https://wildbow.wordpress.com/2017/05/22/thoughts-on-writing-serials/>

## Best-fit stack for long serialized fantasy: Sanderson threads + Wells/Weiland arcs + Harmon chapters

Synthesis across the fetched sources: the Hero's Journey and Save the Cat are single-protagonist, fixed-length templates (Reedsy's Hero's Journey guide explicitly flags ensemble casts as a poor fit; Snyder's percentages assume a bounded 110-page work), so they suit one book, not an open-ended serial. The frameworks that scale are Sanderson's promise/progress/payoff (explicitly designed to run per-subplot in multi-threaded epics), seven-point/Weiland beats applied per arc with a known arc resolution, and Harmon's Story Circle, which was built for episodic return-and-repeat storytelling. Reedsy's overall guidance applies on top: 'story structures aren't an exact science... to break the rules of traditional story structure, you must first understand them.'

Source: Original synthesis by this research; each component individually sourced (Sanderson lectures, Wells/Weiland guides, Harmon circle) -- <https://reedsy.com/blog/guide/story-structure/>

## Caveats

Save the Cat beats came via Reedsy's guide because savethecat.com (the official site) was unreachable (DNS failure); the beat list matches Snyder's book but percentages here are Reedsy's novel-adapted rendering of Snyder's 110-page screenplay page numbers. The three-act structure is often loosely attributed to Aristotle, but Aristotle's Poetics only describes beginning/middle/end; the modern beat-mapped version is a 20th-century screenwriting formalization (Syd Field). Campbell's monomyth is academically contested (critics dispute its claimed universality and note Campbell's sample bias), and stage counts differ by source: Campbell 17, Vogler 12, and the Reedsy Story Circle article says Harmon condensed '11 steps', an inconsistency in the source itself. Dan Wells' seven-point structure is his popularization of a system he credits to the Star Trek RPG rulebook, not an original invention; the Reedsy article does not surface that credit. The GRRM gardener/architect quote is authentic and widely repeated but Goodreads gives no original citation; it traces to multiple interviews so exact first occurrence is unverified. Dan Harmon's own Channel 101 'Story Structure 101' pages were paywalled (HTTP 402) so his framework is cited secondhand via Reedsy. MasterClass and Mythcreants 403'd; no findings rely on them.
