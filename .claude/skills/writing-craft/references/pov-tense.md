# POV and tense

Craft rules distilled from live-fetched sources and adversarially source-verified on 2026-07-11. Every attribution below survived a fact-check pass (refuted or corrected claims were fixed before inclusion). Apply as guidance, not dogma: when a rule fights the story, the story wins.

## Default to tight third-person limited for ensemble fantasy

George R.R. Martin: "I'm a strong believer in telling stories through a limited but very tight third person point of view," and "I actually hate the omniscient viewpoint. None of us have an omniscient viewpoint; we are alone in the universe." Each ASOIAF chapter is locked to one head, and Martin drafts several consecutive chapters of the same character because each POV has a distinct voice and vocabulary that is exhausting to switch between. For a serialized multi-POV epic, this is the industry-standard default: limited third, one named head per chapter, voice recalibrated per character.

Source: George R.R. Martin, long-form interview (Asshai.com interview, reposted) -- <https://ohnotheydidnt.livejournal.com/72570529.html>

## One POV per scene; switch heads only at a scene or chapter break

Head-hopping is when a scene told from one character's POV suddenly includes thoughts or perceptions that character could not know. It weakens emotional attachment to the POV character, breaks immersion, and kills mystery by revealing everyone's thoughts. The mechanical fix: limit each scene to one character's perspective, and mark every POV switch with a scene break (centered # or blank-line divider) or a chapter break, applied consistently across the whole manuscript.

Source: Scribophile Writing Academy, "What Is Head-Hopping?" -- <https://www.scribophile.com/academy/what-is-head-hopping>

## Omniscient is a narrator persona, not permission to head-hop

Legitimate omniscient POV has "a distinct narrator who actively tells the story" in their own voice; head-hopping is accidental perspective drift inside what should be limited POV. If you write omniscient, give the narrator a personality rather than a neutral godlike camera, and accept that limited third is now far more common (omniscient "used to be the norm" but no longer is). An AI skill should therefore flag mid-scene thought-access from multiple heads unless the book has explicitly declared an omniscient narrator with a consistent voice from page one.

Source: Scribophile Writing Academy; Writing Excuses 12.9 Q&A on Viewpoint (Dan Wells, Howard Tayler, Piper J. Drake, et al.) -- <https://writingexcuses.com/12-9-qa-on-viewpoint/>

## Give the scene to the character with the most at stake

When picking which character narrates a scene in a multi-POV book, the Writing Excuses hosts converge on stakes and pain: choose the character who experiences the most pain or has the highest emotional investment, who faces the hardest decision (Dan Wells), or who has the strongest emotional response (Piper J. Drake). Two supporting mechanics: open every new scene by immediately establishing whose head the reader is in, and end POV sections on a hook ("zinger") so the reader tolerates leaving that character.

Source: Writing Excuses podcast, episode 12.9 "Q&A on Viewpoint" (Brandon Sanderson's podcast; hosts Dan Wells, Howard Tayler, guests) -- <https://writingexcuses.com/12-9-qa-on-viewpoint/>

## Choose person by what the story is about: first for character interiority, third limited for cast management

Sanderson's BYU lecture framework: first person buys "strong character voice, immediacy" but is "harder to manage with multiple viewpoints"; limited third buys "credibility, easier to manage large casts, allows for contrast between character perception and reality" at the cost of some intimacy. Dan Wells's heuristic on the same podcast: "Is this story about the plot or the character? If it's about the character, I do it in first person." Genre conventions also weigh in (YA leans first person; adult epic fantasy leans third limited).

Source: Brandon Sanderson, BYU 318 creative writing lectures (2020), community lecture notes; Dan Wells on Writing Excuses 12.9 -- <https://gist.github.com/jake9696/86f1f9d65af275d77435f6b33c78d64b>

## Know Gardner's five-step psychic-distance ladder and place every sentence on it

John Gardner's scale runs from remote to inside-the-skull: (1) "It was winter of the year 1853. A large man stepped out of a doorway." (2) "Henry J. Warburton had never much cared for snowstorms." (3) "Henry hated snowstorms." (4) "God how he hated these damn snowstorms." (free indirect style) (5) "Snow. Under your collar, down inside your shoes..." (stream of consciousness). As distance tightens, the POV character's diction takes over the narration; as it widens, the author's voice returns. Vary distance rhythmically: constant closeness is claustrophobic, constant distance is monotonous; reserve level 4-5 for high-emotion beats and altered states.

Source: John Gardner, The Art of Fiction (1983), as presented by Emma Darwin, "Psychic Distance: What It Is and How to Use It" (This Itch of Writing) -- <https://emmadarwin.substack.com/p/psychic-distance-what-it-is-and-how>

## Zoom gradually: wide for scene-setting, close for pivotal beats, never jump-cut

Treat narrative distance like a camera dolly, not a teleport: move stepwise along Gardner's scale rather than jumping from level 1 to level 5, because abrupt shifts jolt readers out of the story. Use wide distance for establishing scenes, time/space transitions, and giving readers a processing break after intense sequences; narrow distance to make pivotal, emotional moments visceral. This is the framework that filter-word rules serve: stripping filters is how you execute a zoom-in, not a blanket law.

Source: The History Quill (historical-fiction editorial team), "Narrative distance: what it is and how to control it," applying John Gardner's scale -- <https://thehistoryquill.com/narrative-distance-what-it-is-and-how-to-control-it/>

## Strip filter words to close distance, but keep them when the distance is deliberate

Filter words (noticed, seemed, saw, realized, felt, thought, wondered, knew, decided) "increase the narrative distance, reminding us that what we're reading is being told by someone rather than experienced." Cutting them converts "He felt the familiar coldness seeping through his trousers" into "The familiar coldness seeped through his trousers and into his flesh" -- tighter, deeper POV. But Harnby is explicit that filters are "not wrong or right": keep them to pull the reader back on purpose, to mark a character's deliberate act of noticing, or to texture first-person voice. An AI skill should flag filters only where the scene wants closeness.

Source: Louise Harnby (fiction editor), "Filter words in fiction: purposeful inclusion and dramatic restriction" -- <https://www.louiseharnbyproofreader.com/blog/filter-words-in-fiction-purposeful-inclusion-and-dramatic-restriction>

## Past tense is the reader default; choose present only for a reason, not fashion

Philip Pullman urges "young present-tense storytellers... to allow themselves to stand back and show me a wider temporal perspective," arguing present tense feels "claustrophobic, always pressed up against the immediate" and forfeits the full range of English tenses. The editorial takeaway: past tense is what readers unconsciously expect, and present tense is not required for immediacy or suspense (past tense has delivered both for centuries). Present tense earns its keep mainly with strong internal voice and a psychology it genuinely suits.

Source: Philip Pullman (via The Guardian, 2010-09-18, "Philip Pullman calls time on the present tense"), quoted and applied by Eschler Editing, "Hook Critique: Choosing Tense, Part 1" -- <https://eschlerediting.com/hook-critique-choosing-tense-part-1/>

## Budget present tense's real costs: time manipulation, foreknowledge suspense, and reflection

David Jauss's ledger: present tense buys immediacy (the reader experiences change in real time) and grammatical simplicity (narratives tend to use only about four of English's twelve tenses), and can serve theme (Charles Baxter's The Feast of Love uses it for "the presentness of the past"). It costs: flashbacks and chronology feel unnatural, the narrator cannot build suspense from foreknowledge, characters lose the psychological depth that retrospection provides, and the mode invites trivial play-by-play, "less the work of an author than an unedited film." Pick the tense whose costs your book can afford.

Source: David Jauss, On Writing Fiction (2011), excerpted as "The Pros and Cons of Writing a Novel in Present Tense" in Writer's Digest -- <https://www.writersdigest.com/write-better-fiction/the-pros-and-cons-of-writing-a-novel-in-present-tense>

## Any POV or tense shift is a structural event: signal it and rewrite around it

Le Guin names five principal modes (first person, limited third, involved author, detached author, observer-narrator) and warns: "A shift from one point of view to another will affect the whole narrative, and if required, it must be done carefully." Unmarked drift between past and present tense confuses readers the same way; use section breaks or chapter divisions as the cue. She holds up Virginia Woolf as proof that mid-scene shifts CAN work, but only with effortless, perfectly clear transitions -- i.e., masters may break the scene-break rule, drafts should not.

Source: Ursula K. Le Guin, Steering the Craft (1998/2015), chapters on POV and "Changing Point of View," via two independent reviews -- <https://aishwariyalaxmi.com/2025/02/28/review-of-ursula-k-le-guins-steering-the-craft/>

## Prototype the POV/tense with test chapters before committing the book

N.K. Jemisin "tried a number of different test chapters at the beginning of THE FIFTH SEASON to decide what voice/character/centering" to use; "the second person voice just happened to be the one that felt right," and she nearly abandoned the manuscript over doubts about it. Reedsy's guide gives the same operational advice: draft a key scene in two or three candidate POVs and keep the one that reads best against genre conventions. For an AI skill: before chapter 1 is locked, generate the same scene in the candidate POV/tense pairs and choose explicitly; after locking, treat the choice as a contract with the reader.

Source: N.K. Jemisin, Goodreads author Q&A on The Fifth Season; Reedsy point-of-view guide -- <https://www.goodreads.com/questions/1788711-why-did-you-decide-to-use-the-second>

## Fanfic reader-insert has its own POV contract: second person, vague 'you', minimal placeholders

Reader-insert fic "is typically written in 2nd person Point of View by using you and your pronouns," with the reader as protagonist paired with a canon character. Conventions: keep the reader-protagonist's physical details vague so anyone can project (avoid e.g. 'turning red,' which assumes a skin tone); placeholders like Y/N, (F/n), (h/c), (e/c) exist but have no standard and many readers find bracket-heavy fics unreadable; stronger works skip placeholders and use pronouns, titles, or in-story nicknames instead. FanFiction.net banned second-person/reader-insert fic in 2005, while AO3 hosts it under Reader/You tags -- so platform rules matter. These conventions are fanfic-specific and must not leak into original-fiction POV advice.

Source: Fanlore wiki (Organization for Transformative Works), "Reader-Insert" -- <https://fanlore.org/wiki/Reader-Insert>

## Caveats

(1) The "one POV per scene" ban is a modern genre-editorial convention, not a historical law: omniscient with free head movement was the 19th-century norm, and Le Guin cites Virginia Woolf shifting POV mid-scene successfully with clear cues. Skills should enforce it for drafts but recognize declared-omniscient books. (2) Pullman's anti-present-tense position is openly contested: the Guardian's own report noted the headline claim was more "muffled and provisional" in the actual text, and acclaimed present-tense novels (Booker winners among them) keep being published; Jauss himself catalogs genuine advantages. Treat "past is default" as a strong prior, not a ban. The Guardian primary URL returned 403 to direct fetch; Pullman's quotes were verified through two secondary fetches (a search snippet of the Guardian piece and Eschler Editing's quotation). (3) The "give the scene to whoever has most at stake" heuristic is often attributed to Orson Scott Card's Characters and Viewpoint ("the character who hurts"); I could only verify Card's book at study-guide level (BookRags), not the exact quote, so the finding is attributed to Writing Excuses where I verified the wording. (4) Sanderson's lecture content was verified via community lecture notes (near-primary), not an official transcript. (5) Le Guin's exact taxonomy varies slightly between the two reviews fetched (one lists "involved author/detached author/observer-narrator", the other "reliable narrator/omniscient author/objective narrator"), and she reportedly dislikes the term "omniscient" as judgmental; treat mode names loosely. (6) Jemisin explicitly says second person in The Fifth Season simply "felt right" after test chapters; thematic readings (oppression, dissociation) are critics' interpretations, not her stated rule. (7) Scribophile, Reedsy, History Quill, Eschler, and Harnby are editor/service sites (near-primary craft authorities), not author-primary sources.
