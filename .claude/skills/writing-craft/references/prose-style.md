# Prose rules from famous authors

Craft rules distilled from live-fetched sources and adversarially source-verified on 2026-07-11. Every attribution below survived a fact-check pass (refuted or corrected claims were fixed before inclusion). Apply as guidance, not dogma: when a rule fights the story, the story wins.

## King: The adverb is not your friend

Stephen King's most-quoted prose rule: 'I believe the road to hell is paved with adverbs.' The fix is not deletion alone but choosing a stronger verb or letting context carry the meaning; he is harshest on adverbs attached to dialogue attribution ('"Put it down!" she shouted menacingly'). Actionable form: flag every -ly adverb, especially after 'said', and either cut it or replace the verb it props up. King pairs this with a matching warning against timid passive voice: 'Timid writers like passive verbs for the same reason that timid lovers like passive partners.'

Source: Stephen King, On Writing: A Memoir of the Craft (2000) -- <https://www.openculture.com/2014/03/stephen-kings-top-20-rules-for-writers.html>

## King: 2nd Draft = 1st Draft - 10%

King's revision formula, which he got from a rejection slip early in his career: 'Rewrite formula: 2nd Draft = 1st Draft - 10%.' As an editing pass, count the words in the draft and target cutting a tenth, mostly filler phrases, redundant beats, and throat-clearing. For a serialized-chapter workflow this converts revision from a vague 'polish' step into a measurable one: the second pass must be shorter than the first.

Source: Stephen King, On Writing (2000) -- <https://www.azquotes.com/quote/599761>

## King: Write with the door closed, rewrite with the door open

'Write with the door closed, rewrite with the door open.' The first draft is written for yourself, fast and private, with no audience in the room; only in revision do you consider readers and feedback. For books written in the open on GitHub, the operational translation is: draft a chapter without soliciting or reacting to comments, then do the open-door pass (reader feedback, beta notes) before publishing the revision. King's companion revision rule is 'Kill your darlings, kill your darlings, even when it breaks your egocentric little scribbler's heart' -- cut a passage you love if it serves you rather than the story.

Source: Stephen King, On Writing (2000) -- <https://www.goodreads.com/work/quotes/150292-on-writing>

## Elmore Leonard's 10 Rules (the exact list)

1. Never open a book with weather. 2) Avoid prologues. 3) Never use a verb other than 'said' to carry dialogue. 4) Never use an adverb to modify 'said' ('he admonished gravely' is a mortal sin). 5) Keep exclamation points under control: no more than two or three per 100,000 words. 6) Never use the words 'suddenly' or 'all hell broke loose'. 7) Use regional dialect/patois sparingly. 8) Avoid detailed descriptions of characters. 9) Don't go into great detail describing places and things. 10) Try to leave out the part that readers tend to skip (thick paragraphs of prose; readers rarely skip dialogue). His summarizing meta-rule: 'If it sounds like writing, I rewrite it.'

Source: Elmore Leonard, 'Easy on the Adverbs, Exclamation Points and Especially Hooptedoodle', New York Times (2001), later Elmore Leonard's 10 Rules of Writing (2007) -- <https://www.buzzfeednews.com/article/alisonvingiano/elmore-leonards-ten-rules-for-writing-fiction>

## Orwell's six rules for honest prose

From Politics and the English Language (1946): i) Never use a metaphor, simile or other figure of speech which you are used to seeing in print. ii) Never use a long word where a short one will do. iii) If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out. iv) Never use the passive where you can use the active. v) Never use a foreign phrase, a scientific word or a jargon word if you can think of an everyday English equivalent. vi) Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous. He also gives a pre-writing checklist a scrupulous writer asks of every sentence: What am I trying to say? What words will express it? Is this image fresh enough to have an effect? Could I put it more shortly?

Source: George Orwell, 'Politics and the English Language' (1946), via The Orwell Foundation (full primary text) -- <https://www.orwellfoundation.com/the-orwell-foundation/orwell/essays-and-other-works/politics-and-the-english-language/>

## Strunk & White: Omit needless words; prefer active, concrete, positive statements

The core cluster from The Elements of Style: Rule 13, 'Omit needless words. Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences, for the same reason that a drawing should have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts.' Rule 10: 'The active voice is usually more direct and vigorous than the passive.' Rule 11: 'Make definite assertions. Avoid tame, colorless, hesitating, non-committal language.' Rule 12: 'Prefer the specific to the general, the definite to the vague, the concrete to the abstract.' These four convert directly into lint-style checks on any paragraph.

Source: William Strunk Jr., The Elements of Style (1918/1920 primary text; later revised with E.B. White) -- <https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/37134/pg37134.txt>

## Palahniuk: Ban thought verbs; un-pack them into sensory evidence

In 'Nuts and Bolts: Thought Verbs' Palahniuk assigns six months of writing without: thinks, knows, understands, realizes, believes, wants, remembers, imagines, desires, plus loves, hates, and (largely) is and has. Thought verbs are shortcuts; instead 'un-pack' them into specific sensory detail: action, smell, taste, sound, feeling. His example: instead of 'Kenny wondered if Monica didn't like him going out at night', show her pretending to sleep, making coffee only for herself, the combination lock still warm from her body. Two corollaries: never leave a character alone (solitary characters lapse into thinking; 'get your character with another character, fast'), and embed physical description in action ('Ann coughed and waved one hand past her face... blue eyes, before she smiled' rather than 'Ann's eyes are blue').

Source: Chuck Palahniuk, 'Nuts and Bolts: Thought Verbs', LitReactor essay (2013), reproduced at DocsLib (original LitReactor site is offline) -- <https://docslib.org/doc/13908564/nuts-and-bolts-thought-verbs>

## Le Guin: The test of a sentence is, does it sound right

From Steering the Craft: 'The sound of the language is where it all begins. The test of a sentence is, Does it sound right?' Le Guin says pace and movement 'depend above all on rhythm, and the primary way you feel and control the rhythm of your prose is by hearing it -- by listening to it', so read every passage aloud (or with the 'mind's ear'); faults readers call 'dull, choppy, droning' are sound faults. Her exercises: write a paragraph built for oral performance using onomatopoeia, alliteration and repetition (prose, not verse), and write an action where the sentence rhythm physically embodies the motion described. Practical rule for an assistant: an editing pass that only checks meaning is incomplete; check cadence and sentence-length variety by ear.

Source: Ursula K. Le Guin, Steering the Craft: A Twenty-First-Century Guide to Sailing the Sea of Story (2015 revision), excerpted at Literary Hub -- <https://lithub.com/a-writing-lesson-from-ursula-k-leguin/>

## Vonnegut's 8 rules for (short) fiction

From the Bagombo Snuff Box introduction: 1) Use the time of a total stranger in such a way that he or she will not feel the time was wasted. 2) Give the reader at least one character he or she can root for. 3) Every character should want something, even if it is only a glass of water. 4) Every sentence must do one of two things: reveal character or advance the action. 5) Start as close to the end as possible. 6) Be a sadist: make awful things happen to your leading characters so the reader sees what they are made of. 7) Write to please just one person. 8) Give your readers as much information as possible as soon as possible; 'to heck with suspense' -- readers should be able to finish the story themselves 'should cockroaches eat the last few pages.' Vonnegut immediately adds that great writers break these rules, naming Flannery O'Connor, who broke practically all of them except the first.

Source: Kurt Vonnegut, introduction to Bagombo Snuff Box (1999) -- <https://huntingthemuse.net/library/vonnegut-8-creative-writing-rules>

## Show, don't tell -- but telling is a tool, not a sin

Showing renders emotion as observable behavior ('Michael tensed... gripped the sheets, and held his breath' vs 'Michael was terribly afraid of the dark'), and it is the default for emotional peaks and key scenes. But 'it's certainly necessary to have some telling in every story': telling is correct for conveying essential setup information quickly, clarifying details after a reveal, compressing time via summary, and keeping pace when immersive description would stall momentum. Actionable rule: show what the scene turns on; tell what merely connects scenes. Skill lies in choosing per beat, not in obeying the maxim globally.

Source: Reedsy editorial team, 'Show, Don't Tell: Tips and Examples of The Golden Rule' (Reedsy blog) -- <https://reedsy.com/blog/show-dont-tell/>

## Murder your darlings (correct source: Quiller-Couch, 1914)

The original: 'Whenever you feel an impulse to perpetrate a piece of exceptionally fine writing, obey it -- whole-heartedly -- and delete it before sending your manuscript to press. Murder your darlings.' The target is not any beloved passage but self-consciously fine writing, prose that exists to display the author. As an editing rule: when a sentence's main job is to be admired, cut it. The line is routinely misattributed to Faulkner, Wilde, Welty, Chesterton, Chekhov, or Stephen King; King only popularized his own restatement in On Writing.

Source: Arthur Quiller-Couch, 'On Style' lecture, On the Art of Writing (Cambridge, 1914); attribution history via Forrest Wickman, Slate (2013) -- <https://slate.com/culture/2013/10/kill-your-darlings-writing-advice-what-writer-really-said-to-murder-your-babies.html>

## The Chekhov 'glint on broken glass' quote is a paraphrase, not a quotation

Quote Investigator concludes Chekhov never wrote 'Don't tell me the moon is shining; show me the glint of light on broken glass.' What he actually wrote (letter to his brother Alexander, May 1886) was advice to 'seize on small details': if you write that on the mill dam a piece of glass from a broken bottle glittered like a bright little star, the reader will see a moonlit night. The snappy imperative version is an editorial condensation that first appears in print around 2002. The underlying rule survives and is actionable: convey atmosphere through one specific, luminous detail rather than a general declaration.

Source: Anton Chekhov, letter to Alexander Chekhov (May 1886); paraphrase history traced by Garson O'Toole, Quote Investigator (2013) -- <https://quoteinvestigator.com/2013/07/30/moon-glint/>

## Caveats

Misattributions confirmed by dedicated fact-checks: the Chekhov 'moon/broken glass' line is a ~2002 paraphrase of an 1886 letter (Quote Investigator), and 'kill/murder your darlings' is Quiller-Couch 1914, not Faulkner/Wilde/King (Slate). Several rules are self-consciously breakable by their own authors: Orwell's rule vi and Vonnegut's Flannery O'Connor note both say great writers violate the lists; Leonard framed his rules as tricks for his own invisible style, and rules 8-9 (minimal description) directly conflict with the expectations of epic fantasy readers, so apply them per-genre. King's adverb ban is widely contested as absolutist in modern craft commentary (he uses adverbs himself); treat it as 'default to a stronger verb', not zero tolerance. Strunk & White's 'active voice' rule is criticized by linguists (e.g. Geoffrey Pullum) as overbroad -- I did not fetch that critique, so it is unverified here. Source-quality notes: the Guardian's canonical Leonard piece, MasterClass, LitReactor (domain dead), Gotham Writers, and web.archive.org were unreachable from this environment, so Leonard's list is cited via BuzzFeed News' reproduction, Palahniuk via a DocsLib reproduction cross-checked against a WordPress account, and Vonnegut via huntingthemuse.net (matches the well-attested Bagombo text). King's 'minus 10%' formula wording comes from AZQuotes (weak source) but is corroborated by the stacker.news search hit; the exact 'dress up the vocabulary' quote was never fetched and is omitted from findings.
