# Pacing and scene construction

Craft rules distilled from live-fetched sources and adversarially source-verified on 2026-07-11. Every attribution below survived a fact-check pass (refuted or corrected claims were fixed before inclusion). Apply as guidance, not dogma: when a rule fights the story, the story wins.

## Open every scene with a concrete goal, block it with conflict, end it in disaster

Dwight Swain's Scene unit has exactly three parts: Goal (what the POV character wants at the scene's start, specific and clearly defined), Conflict (obstacles while pursuing it), and Disaster (the scene must end in failure). Ingermanson is blunt about the ending: 'Don't give him the Goal! Winning is boring!' Practical application: before drafting a scene, write one line each for goal, obstacle, and the setback that ends it; if you can't name the goal, the scene has no engine and should be cut or merged.

Source: Dwight Swain's Scene-Sequel model (Techniques of the Selling Writer, 1965), as taught by Randy Ingermanson, 'Writing the Perfect Scene' -- <https://www.advancedfictionwriting.com/articles/writing-the-perfect-scene/>

## Follow each scene with a sequel (reaction, dilemma, decision) and use sequel length as your pacing throttle

After a Scene's disaster comes a Sequel with three ordered parts: Reaction (the emotional response to the setback), Dilemma ('a situation with no good options'), and Decision (the character picks among imperfect options and becomes proactive again). The Decision produces the Goal of the next scene, which is what chains scenes into forward motion instead of episodic drift. Sequels are where the reader breathes; compressing or cutting them speeds the book up, expanding them slows it down, so treat sequel length as a deliberate pacing dial rather than filler.

Source: Reaction-Dilemma-Decision: Randy Ingermanson, 'Writing the Perfect Scene'; sequel length as pacing throttle: Dwight Swain, Techniques of the Selling Writer (1965) -- <https://www.advancedfictionwriting.com/articles/writing-the-perfect-scene/>

## Write moment-to-moment prose as Motivation-Reaction Units: stimulus first, then feeling, reflex, rational action

Swain's small-scale unit alternates an objective Motivation (external stimulus, shown 'like a videocamera') with the character's Reaction, whose parts must come in strict physiological order: feeling, then reflex action, then rational action or speech. Violating the order (character speaks calmly before registering the punch) breaks immersion; presenting reaction before stimulus confuses the reader. This is one of the most mechanically checkable pacing rules: audit any beat that feels 'off' for stimulus-response inversion.

Source: Dwight Swain via Randy Ingermanson, 'Writing the Perfect Scene' -- <https://www.advancedfictionwriting.com/articles/writing-the-perfect-scene/>

## Chain scenes like dominoes: each disaster feeds the next goal, because 'no conflict, no story'

K.M. Weiland frames the same Scene (goal, conflict, disaster) / Sequel (reaction, dilemma, decision) split as a cause-and-effect chain where each scene's disaster triggers the next scene's goal through the sequel's decision, 'each one knocking into the next.' The actionable test: for every chapter, be able to state what previous event caused it and what it causes next; a scene that could be removed without breaking the chain is a pacing dead spot.

Source: K.M. Weiland, 'How to Structure Scenes in Your Story', Helping Writers Become Authors -- <https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/how-to-structure-scenes/>

## Aim for 2,000-5,000 word chapters; fantasy runs longer, and vary length like musical rhythm

Reedsy's guideline for adult fiction is 2,000-4,000 words per chapter, with novels averaging 2,000-5,000; 'fantasy novels often have longer chapters, while shorter chapter lengths are more frequent in thrillers.' Short chapters create a staccato, brisk feel; long ones sustain suspended tension; but too-short chapters bleed momentum and overlong ones lose readers. Treat these as defaults to deviate from deliberately (Reedsy cites The Luminaries' 360-page opening chapter as a successful outlier).

Source: Reedsy blog, 'How Long Should a Chapter Be?' -- <https://reedsy.com/blog/how-long-should-a-chapter-be/>

## For serialized web fiction: bank a 12-16 chapter backlog and rank consistency over frequency

Wildbow (Worm, ~1.7M words published twice weekly) recommends writing 12-16 chapters before publishing anything, both to survive life interruptions and to discover your sustainable cadence -- he planned daily chapters and learned from the backlog that twice weekly was realistic. His three pillars are consistency, frequency, and quality, in that order: 'One post a month is consistent, it's 29.2 days between each update. That's a lot of time for readers to forget you exist.' He also warns that posting too often 'might demand softer cliffhangers or too many cliffhangers, making the story too fragmented' -- cliffhanger density is a pacing budget, not a free lever.

Source: Wildbow (John C. McCrae, author of Worm), 'Thoughts on Writing Serials', Pig's Pen blog (2017) -- <https://wildbow.wordpress.com/2017/05/22/thoughts-on-writing-serials/>

## Try to leave out the part that readers tend to skip

Elmore Leonard's rule 10, capped by his meta-rule 'If it sounds like writing, I rewrite it,' targets the passages that stall momentum: long descriptive blocks, weather openings, and self-conscious prose. For revision, identify stretches with no goal, no new information, and no tension change, and cut or compress them to summary. Note the Novlr piece explicitly calls the rules 'contentious' and genre-specific (rules 8-9 on description fit Leonard's lean crime style poorly for fantasy), so apply rule 10 as the universal core and the description rules with judgment.

Source: Elmore Leonard, 'Ten Rules of Writing' (originally a 2001 New York Times essay), discussed by Pamela Koehne-Drube at Novlr -- <https://www.novlr.org/the-reading-room/elmore-leonards-10-rules-for-writing-how-useful-are-they>

## Vary sentence length deliberately -- write music, not a monotone

Gary Provost's famous demonstration ('This sentence has five words. Here are five more words...') shows that unbroken same-length sentences deaden prose, while 'a combination of short, medium, and long sentences' creates 'a sound that pleases the reader's ear.' As a working rule: short sentences accelerate and hit hard; long sentences slow, sweep, and build; a paragraph of uniform lengths in either direction is a tempo bug. Check drafts for three-plus consecutive sentences of similar length in high-variation passages.

Source: Gary Provost, 100 Ways to Improve Your Writing (1985), quoted at Goodreads -- <https://www.goodreads.com/author/quotes/152930.Gary_Provost>

## Put micro-tension on every page: tension is not the same thing as conflict

Donald Maass's principle 'tension on every page' works at the sentence and word level, not the plot level -- it is not 'battles, runaway trains, smoking guns' but unease built from word choice, conflicting emotions, and specific imagery even in quiet scenes. Fox's exercise: take any paragraph, strengthen its verbs, nouns, and metaphors until it carries emotional charge, then repeat across the page; 'you can always ratchet up the tension, no matter how tense your story already is.' This is the answer to 'no boring parts': quiet chapters earn their place through micro-tension, not by borrowing plot conflict they don't have.

Source: Donald Maass (Writing the Breakout Novel / Writing 21st Century Fiction), summarized by novelist Janet Fox, 'What Is Micro-Tension?' -- <https://janetfox.substack.com/p/what-is-micro-tension>

## In action scenes: shorten sentences, strip conjunctions, ban speeches, and insert micro-resets

Concrete action-pacing mechanics: 'Long sentences slow the reader down. Short sentences speed them up' -- fragment sentences at peak intensity and replace 'and'/'but' with periods to create staccato beats. Keep choreography causal and answerable ('Who initiates? What is the physical action? What is the physical response? What changes as a result?'), cut exposition and plans mid-combat ('Characters in real danger do not make speeches'; dialogue functional, interrupted, under five words a line), and break sustained danger with brief regroup beats because 'a scene that runs at maximum intensity for ten pages exhausts the reader.' Prefer accessible language over technical fight terminology, and ground stakes in what the character personally loses.

Source: Squibler writing guide, 'How to Write Great Action Scenes' -- <https://www.squibler.io/learn/writing/how-to-write-action-scenes/>

## Mark time skips explicitly, then re-anchor the reader within the first lines

Handle elapsed time with a structural marker (white space / scene break / chapter break) plus an explicit anchor ('Six months had passed since the funeral'), or embed the passage in concrete changed details ('a planted sapling in one chapter could be a fully grown tree'). Immediately after the jump, ground the reader in the new time, place, and emotional state before advancing plot, and filter the skip through the POV character's perception of whether it dragged or flew. For mid-length gaps, compress with narrative summary instead of skipping, so essential off-page events still register.

Source: Novlr Reading Room, 'How do you write time skips?' -- <https://www.novlr.org/the-reading-room/how-do-you-write-time-skips>

## Signpost progress in every chapter -- readers quit when they can't feel movement

Sanderson's promise/progress/payoff framework distinguishes the Big-P plot from 'little p' chapter-by-chapter progression, and warns: 'A big reason that people drop off a book is that there weren't enough signposts of progress.' Progress need not be event progress -- it can be information (mystery clues), relationship movement, or internal growth -- but each chapter should leave a visible breadcrumb that the story advanced toward a promised payoff, defined as 'a surprising but fulfilling answer to your promise.' For serialized fiction this is critical: every installment must land at least one signpost.

Source: Brandon Sanderson, 2025 BYU creative writing lecture #2 (Guide to Plot), brandonsanderson.com -- <https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/brandon-sandersons-2025-guide-to-plot-lecture-2>

## Caveats

Leonard's ten rules are widely treated as gospel but the fetched Novlr analysis calls them 'contentious' and genre-specific; rules 8-9 (minimal description) suit his lean crime style and translate poorly to secondary-world fantasy, so only rule 10 ('leave out the part that readers tend to skip') should be enforced universally. The rules' primary source (2001 NYT essay 'Easy on the Adverbs...') is paywalled/unfetchable, as were Guardian and Writer's Digest reprints, so exact hooptedoodle wording is via secondary sources. The Provost passage is real and from 100 Ways to Improve Your Writing (1985), but the fetched source is Goodreads (aggregator), not the book itself. Web-fiction chapter-length numbers (Royal Road typical 1,500-3,500 words; one data analysis claiming 4,000-8,000-word chapters rate highest; ~2,500-5,000 for genre fiction) came from search-result snippets because the Royal Road forum threads and chapterchronicles.com themselves returned 403/DNS errors -- treat those specific numbers as plausible but unverified. Swain's Scene-Sequel model is summarized via Ingermanson (near-primary, and the standard modern reference), not Swain's 1965 book directly. Two additional corroborating fetches not used as finding anchors: Reedsy's fight-scene guide (fights should read in roughly real time, 1-2 pages) and RaeAnne Thayne at Writer's Digest (alternate tension and release; 'deploy cliffhangers at pivotal moments only'). MasterClass and Writer Unboxed pages 403'd, so Maass's micro-tension is cited through a novelist's summary rather than his publisher's page.
