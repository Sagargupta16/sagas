# Perspective essay craft

Craft rules distilled from live-fetched sources and adversarially source-verified on 2026-07-11. Every attribution below survived a fact-check pass (refuted or corrected claims were fixed before inclusion). Apply as guidance, not dogma: when a rule fights the story, the story wins.

## Start from a question, not a thesis

Graham says a real essay is an attempt (from French 'essayer', to try): "you can't begin with a thesis, because you don't have one, and may never have one. An essay doesn't begin with a statement, but with a question... You notice a door that's ajar, and you open it and walk in to see what's inside." Practical rule for the sagas essays shelf: open each perspective piece with the genuine question that prompted it, and let the writing discover the answer rather than defending a pre-chosen position. Graham also warns that essays written purely for oneself "peter out" -- writing for an audience forces you to think well.

Source: Paul Graham, "The Age of the Essay" (September 2004) -- <https://paulgraham.com/essay.html>

## Flow interesting: meander like a river, then ink over the pencil

Graham's drafting algorithm: "At each step, flow down. For the essayist this translates to: flow interesting. Of all the places to go next, choose the most interesting." When a thread dies, backtrack like a river does (he reports going back seven paragraphs and restarting in another direction). The finished essay is "a cleaned-up train of thought, as dialogue is cleaned-up conversation": cut and fill to emphasize the central thread "like an illustrator inking over a pencil drawing," but "don't change so much that you lose the spontaneity of the original. Err on the side of the river."

Source: Paul Graham, "The Age of the Essay" (September 2004) -- <https://paulgraham.com/essay.html>

## Aim for maximum surprise; use yourself as the reader's proxy

"Essays should aim for maximum surprise" -- surprises are "things that you not only didn't know, but that contradict things you thought you knew," the most valuable facts you can deliver. The test: "You should only write about things you've thought about a lot. And anything you come across that surprises you, who've thought about the topic a lot, will probably surprise most readers." Actionable check for an AI assistant: before publishing, ask what in this essay would surprise someone who already holds the conventional view; if nothing, it is not ready.

Source: Paul Graham, "The Age of the Essay" (September 2004) -- <https://paulgraham.com/essay.html>

## Useful writing = importance x novelty x correctness x strength

Graham scores useful writing on four multiplied components: it must matter to enough people (importance), tell them something new or "something they knew unconsciously but had never put into words" (novelty), be true (correctness), and be stated as strongly as truth allows (strength). Avoid the cheap trick of vagueness: "It's easy to make a statement correct by making it vague." Instead make the strongest claim you can without crossing into falsehood, and use qualifiers as precision instruments for your degree of certainty, not as hedges: "Don't underestimate qualification. It's an important skill in its own right." His revision loop is "loose, then tight": draft freely, then "if you write a bad sentence, you don't publish it. You delete it and try again."

Source: Paul Graham, "How to Write Usefully" (February 2020) -- <https://paulgraham.com/useful.html>

## Know which of Orwell's four motives is driving the piece

Orwell names four great motives present in every prose writer in varying proportions: (i) sheer egoism ("desire to seem clever, to be talked about... It is humbug to pretend this is not a motive"), (ii) aesthetic enthusiasm (pleasure in "the firmness of good prose or the rhythm of a good story"), (iii) historical impulse ("desire to see things as they are, to find out true facts and store them up for the use of posterity"), and (iv) political purpose in the widest sense ("desire to push the world in a certain direction"). His diagnostic is directly usable as a revision check: "it is invariably where I lacked a political purpose that I wrote lifeless books and was betrayed into purple passages, sentences without meaning, decorative adjectives and humbug generally." And the famous style target: "one can write nothing readable unless one constantly struggles to efface one's own personality. Good prose is like a windowpane."

Source: George Orwell, "Why I Write" (Gangrel, 1946), via The Orwell Foundation's full text -- <https://www.orwellfoundation.com/the-orwell-foundation/orwell/essays-and-other-works/why-i-write/>

## Apply Orwell's six rules in revision; let the meaning choose the word

Orwell's checklist for when instinct fails: (i) never use a figure of speech you are used to seeing in print; (ii) never use a long word where a short one will do; (iii) if it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out; (iv) never use the passive where you can use the active; (v) never use a jargon word if an everyday English equivalent exists; (vi) break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous. Upstream of the rules: "What is above all needed is to let the meaning choose the word, and not the other way about" -- think the idea through in pictures and sensations first, then hunt for exact words, because ready-made phrases "anaesthetize a portion of one's brain." "The great enemy of clear language is insincerity."

Source: George Orwell, "Politics and the English Language" (Horizon, April 1946), via The Orwell Foundation's full text -- <https://www.orwellfoundation.com/the-orwell-foundation/orwell/essays-and-other-works/politics-and-the-english-language/>

## Turn yourself into a character: specific, flawed, never the regular Joe

Lopate insists the personal essayist must build a distinct on-page persona out of their own idiosyncrasies, because "who wants to read about that bland creature, the regular Joe?" The narrator must be "knowable enough in their broad outlines to behave 'believably', at the same time as free willed enough to intrigue us with surprises." Practical rule: mine your quirks, flaws, and contradictions and put them on the page early, so the reader has a specific human to travel with; generic first-person narration is a defect, not neutrality.

Source: Phillip Lopate, "On the Necessity of Turning Oneself Into a Character" (from To Show and to Tell, 2013), quoted in Pamela Leavey's Writers on Writing series -- <https://pamelaleavey.com/2021/08/16/writers-on-writing-phillip-lopate/>

## Separate the situation from the story; the persona serves the insight

Gornick's core distinction: "Every work of literature has both a situation and a story. The situation is the context or circumstance, sometimes the plot; the story is the emotional experience that preoccupies the writer: the insight, the wisdom, the thing one has come to say." A personal essay fails when it only narrates the situation. In the best personal narratives "the writer was possessed of an insight that organized the writing, and in each case a persona had been created to serve the insight" -- the twin apprenticeship is knowing "not only why one is speaking but who is speaking." Rule for drafting: state the story (the insight) in one sentence before writing; every scene from the situation must serve it.

Source: Vivian Gornick, The Situation and the Story: The Art of Personal Narrative (2001), quoted at length in The Marginalian -- <https://www.themarginalian.org/2015/06/22/vivian-gornick-the-situation-and-the-story-personal-narrative/>

## Essay voice is unsurrogated: no fictional mask to hide behind

Gornick names the key difference between essay voice and fiction voice: "A novel or a poem provides invented characters or speaking voices that act as surrogates for the writer... The persona in a nonfiction narrative is an unsurrogated one." The essayist must openly own the defenses and embarrassments a novelist can pour into characters, and transform "low-level self-interest into the kind of detached empathy required of a piece of writing that is to be of value to the disinterested reader" -- self-exposure without the whining that "makes the analysand a bore to all the world but the analyst." The reader must also be persuaded "that the narrator is reliable." Rule: in essays, confess your stake and your bias directly; in fiction, distribute them into characters.

Source: Vivian Gornick, The Situation and the Story (2001), quoted in The Marginalian -- <https://www.themarginalian.org/2015/06/22/vivian-gornick-the-situation-and-the-story-personal-narrative/>

## Op-ed skeleton: lede on a hook, evidence-backed argument, 'to be sure', circle back

The OpEd Project's structure: (1) a lede that "sets the scene and grabs your reader's attention," usually built around a news hook that makes the piece timely -- "Be bold, but incontrovertible. Tell an anecdote if it illustrates your point"; (2) a statement of argument, explicit or implied, "based on evidence (such as stats, news, reports from credible organizations, expert quotes, scholarship, history, first-hand experience)"; (3) roughly three evidence points, the third often widening to the bigger picture; (4) a "To Be Sure" paragraph "in which you pre-empt your potential critics by acknowledging any flaws in your argument, and address any obvious counter-arguments"; (5) a conclusion that "often circles back to your lede." Their credibility bar for earning the opinion: "you do need to be well positioned to speak on your topic" and your argument must rest on material "acknowledged as credible even by those who may disagree with your interpretation."

Source: The OpEd Project, "Op-ed Writing: The Basics" -- <https://www.theopedproject.org/oped-basics>

## Steelman before you strike (Rapoport's rules)

Dennett's synthesis of Anatol Rapoport's rules for critical commentary: (1) re-express your target's position "so clearly, vividly, and fairly that your target says, 'Thanks, I wish I'd thought of putting it that way'"; (2) list any points of agreement, especially non-obvious ones; (3) mention anything you have learned from your target; (4) "Only then are you permitted to say so much as a word of rebuttal or criticism." Dennett frames this not as politeness but strategy: it "transforms your opponent into a more receptive audience for your criticism." Pairs with Graham's disagreement hierarchy, where the strongest form (DH6) is refuting the central point: "The author's main point seems to be x. As he says: <quotation>. But this is wrong for the following reasons..." -- refuting minor points or tone is a weaker, often dishonest substitute.

Source: Daniel Dennett, Intuition Pumps and Other Tools for Thinking (2013), summarized with quotes by Maria Popova; Paul Graham, "How to Disagree" (March 2008) -- <https://www.themarginalian.org/2014/03/28/daniel-dennett-rapoport-rules-criticism/>

## Strip every sentence to its cleanest components; ask 'what am I trying to say?'

Zinsser's revision doctrine: "Clutter is the disease of American writing" and "the secret of good writing is to strip every sentence to its cleanest components." Because "clear thinking becomes clear writing," the writer must constantly ask "What am I trying to say?" -- surprisingly often they don't know. Concrete cut list: words that serve no function, adverbs duplicating the verb, weak qualifiers, passive constructions that hide the actor, and near-synonym pileups where one exact word would do. "A clear sentence is no accident" -- clarity is a product of deliberate rewriting, not a first-draft property.

Source: William Zinsser, On Writing Well, ch. 2 "Simplicity" (1976; 30th anniversary ed. 2006), full chapter hosted by UMass Amherst -- <https://websites.umass.edu/wwclement/simplicity/>

## Caveats

Several fetches used curl because the sandbox's WebFetch DNS failed for paulgraham.com, orwellfoundation.com, and theopedproject.org; the quoted text was extracted from the raw HTML of those pages, so quotes are verified verbatim. Two findings rely on near-primary quoting rather than the primary text itself: the Lopate finding quotes short excerpts of his essay via a secondary blog (the full essay PDF hosts were unreachable), and the Gornick finding uses The Marginalian's long direct block quotes from her book (the Paris Review interview returned a 403/redirect). Zinsser detail beyond the three famous quotes came from an AI-summarized fetch of the UMass-hosted chapter, so paraphrases there are faithful but not all verbatim-checked. Contested/misattributed points: Orwell's 'good prose is like a windowpane' is often quoted as a simple minimalism slogan, but in context it is about effacing personality, and he immediately undercuts pure high-mindedness ('All writers are vain, selfish, and lazy'); his six rules also end with rule vi explicitly licensing breaking the other five. The op-ed 'to be sure' paragraph is a newsroom convention, and The OpEd Project itself flags its structure as 'not a rule... just one way of approaching it.' Rapoport's rules are Dennett's synthesis of Rapoport's ideas, not a verbatim Rapoport text. Graham's usefulness components are his own informal heuristic ('like numbers you can multiply'), not a validated formula.
