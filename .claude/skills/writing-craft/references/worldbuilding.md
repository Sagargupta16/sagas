# Worldbuilding for fantasy

Craft rules distilled from live-fetched sources and adversarially source-verified on 2026-07-11. Every attribution below survived a fact-check pass (refuted or corrected claims were fixed before inclusion). Apply as guidance, not dogma: when a rule fights the story, the story wins.

## Sanderson's First Law: magic may only solve problems in proportion to reader understanding

Exact wording: "An author's ability to solve conflict with magic is DIRECTLY PROPORTIONAL to how well the reader understands said magic." This defines the hard/soft spectrum: hard magic has explicit rules so "the reader can have the fun of feeling like they themselves are part of the magic" and characters may solve plot problems with it; soft magic (Tolkien's Gandalf) exists to create wonder, and "the really good writers of soft magic systems very, very rarely use their magic to solve problems in their books." Practical rule for a chapter: before magic resolves a conflict, verify the mechanism was established on the page earlier; inventing new rules mid-crisis "will end up creating fiction that is not only unfulfilling and unexciting, but just plain bad" (deus ex machina).

Source: Brandon Sanderson, essay "Sanderson's First Law" (2007), brandonsanderson.com -- <https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/sandersons-first-law>

## Sanderson's Second Law: limitations are more interesting than powers (Limitations > Powers)

Exact wording: "Limitations > Powers." Sanderson argues "Superman is not his powers. Superman is his weaknesses" and "It is not what the heroes can do that is most important to who they are, but what they have trouble doing." When designing a magic system or a magical character, spend the design budget on costs, weaknesses, and things the magic cannot do; "Originality... doesn't come so much with the power itself as with the limitation." Actionable check: for every power you give a character, write down at least one cost or limit that will actively block them at a plot-critical moment.

Source: Brandon Sanderson, essay "Sanderson's Second Law" (2012), brandonsanderson.com -- <https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/sandersons-second-law>

## Sanderson's Third Law: expand what you already have before you add something new

Exact wording: "Expand what you already have before you add something new." A brilliant system is "less often one with a thousand different powers" and more often one "with relatively few powers that the author has considered in depth." He gives three techniques: Extrapolate (ask "what happens when" the magic touches society, culture, economics), Interconnect (tie powers and themes together rather than presenting a grab-bag of abilities), and Streamline (reuse existing elements, e.g. have different cultures respond differently to the same magic instead of inventing a new magic per culture). For a serialized book this means new chapters should deepen established mechanics rather than introduce fresh ones.

Source: Brandon Sanderson, essay "Sanderson's Third Law of Magic" (2013), brandonsanderson.com -- <https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/sandersons-third-law-of-magic>

## Iceberg principle: show one-eighth, know (or imply) the rest

Hemingway, in Death in the Afternoon: "The dignity of movement of an ice-berg is due to only one-eighth of it being above water." A writer who knows their subject may deliberately omit details, and if the writing is authentic the reader "will have a feeling of those things as strongly as though the writer had stated them"; but omitting things out of ignorance leaves hollow spots. Applied to fantasy: reveal only the world details the scene needs and let the rest press against the surface as implication. Cut every paragraph of lore that exists to prove you did the homework.

Source: Ernest Hemingway, Death in the Afternoon (1932), via Wikipedia's sourced article on the iceberg theory -- <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iceberg_theory>

## Depth over density: interweave world elements instead of accumulating them

From Sanderson's 2025 BYU worldbuilding lecture: "It's not about density of ideas. It's about how the ideas influence each other." He organizes worldbuilding into three pillars (physical setting, cultural setting, magic system) and advises that "readers should sense that the world is vast and complex, but they don't need to see every detail" -- the illusion of depth, not exhaustive depth. Actionable rule: when adding any world element (a religion, a coin, a holiday), immediately connect it to at least one existing element (the magic, the geography, the politics) rather than leaving it freestanding.

Source: Brandon Sanderson, BYU 2025 lecture "Worldbuilding Tools", brandonsanderson.com -- <https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/worldbuilding-tools-lecture-2025>

## Tolkien's standard: a Secondary World must obey its own consistent laws

In "On Fairy-Stories" (1939 lecture, published 1947) Tolkien frames the fantasy author as a "sub-creator" building a "secondary world" that the reader's mind can enter, where the story must be "presented as 'true'" within that world's own rational rules -- "a coherent and organic whole in which all the parts are harmoniously interrelated." The moment the world contradicts itself, belief collapses and the reader is ejected. Practical rule: internal consistency outranks realism; you may posit dragons, but once posited, dragons must behave the same way in chapter 40 as in chapter 2.

Source: J.R.R. Tolkien, "On Fairy-Stories" (1947), summarized with scholarship on Wikipedia -- <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On_Fairy-Stories>

## Know whether you are a gardener or an architect, and compensate accordingly

George R.R. Martin: "There are some writers who are architects... and then there are gardeners who dig a little hole and drop a seed in... I am much more a gardener than an architect." Architects plan the world and plot up front (Tolkien built languages and millennia of history first); gardeners plant a premise and discover the world while drafting. Neither is wrong, but each has a failure mode: architects over-plan and never draft; gardeners create continuity errors in serialized work. For a public serialized project, gardeners especially need the series-bible discipline below, because published chapters cannot be retconned.

Source: George R.R. Martin, Geekson audio interview, Episode 54 (2006-08-04), via Wikiquote -- <https://en.wikiquote.org/wiki/George_R._R._Martin>

## Ask the Aragorn's-tax-policy questions about power and daily governance

Martin's famous critique of Tolkien (Rolling Stone, 2014): "What was Aragorn's tax policy? Did he maintain a standing army? What did he do in times of flood and famine?" -- his point being that 'he ruled wisely' is a sentence, not a world, and grappling with how power actually operates generates story. As a design prompt it is excellent: for any ruler, faction, or institution in your world, be able to answer where the money comes from, who enforces the rules, and what happens in a crisis. Note the critique itself is contested (see caveats): the value is in asking the questions, not in believing Tolkien failed.

Source: George R.R. Martin, Rolling Stone interview (2014), quoted and analyzed by Liz Bourke -- <https://lizbourke.wordpress.com/2026/02/19/137-aragorns-tax-policy-and-other-weird-shibboleths/>

## Cure worldbuilder's disease: build only what the next scene needs

Worldbuilder's disease is when "a writer gets so caught up in creating their world that it becomes more important to them than storytelling." Symptoms: mistaking accumulated lore wordcount for progress, info-dumping ("you'll want to showcase every aspect of it any chance you get"), and using worldbuilding as procrastination from drafting. The cure is demand-driven worldbuilding: "Think of what your scenes need first," alternate macro and micro setting detail for pacing, and start drafting because "the story living in your mind will never exist until you write it." Sanderson's version of the same advice: set deadlines, or "you could spend so long on this that you get paralyzed."

Source: Cole Salao, 'Worldbuilder's Disease: How to Recover and Actually Finish Your Story', TCK Publishing -- <https://www.tckpublishing.com/worldbuilders-disease/>

## Design cultures, economies, and religions with a structured questionnaire, answered lazily

Patricia C. Wrede's "Fantasy Worldbuilding Questions" (the SFWA-hosted classic) organizes world design into interrogable categories: Peoples and Customs (language, ethics, values), Religion and the Gods, Social Organization (government, politics, foreign relations), Commerce/Trade/Public Life (business, transportation, communication), and Daily Life (diet, fashion, education, calendars). Crucially, Wrede says it is "not necessary for an author to answer all... questions in order to start writing" -- it is a thinking tool, not homework. Rule: when a chapter touches an institution (a temple, a market, a court), answer that section's questions just-in-time and record the answers in the series bible.

Source: Patricia C. Wrede, "Fantasy Worldbuilding Questions" (originally circulated 1996, hosted by SFWA and on her site) -- <https://pcwrede.com/pcw-wp/fantasy-worldbuilding-questions/>

## Give each culture one sound palette; pass the mouth-feel test; no decorative apostrophes

Per-culture naming rules: establish 3-5 dominant consonants and preferred vowels per culture (a culture favoring L, R, N, TH yields 'Theralan', 'Norith'), so "if your readers can hear a name and guess which culture it belongs to, you've built something that functions the way real language does." Say every name aloud five times in a sentence -- "every time a reader stumbles over a name, they're pulled out of the dream." Apostrophes must mark a real sound (a glottal stop), not exoticism: "a name with an unexplained apostrophe reads like a bumper sticker that says 'I write fantasy.'" Also vary first letters across major characters so readers skimming on a phone never confuse two names.

Source: Loreteller, "How to Create Fantasy Names That Sound Right" (craft guide) -- <https://loreteller.com/learn/fantasy-naming-guide/>

## Keep a series bible from chapter one and update it as you publish

A story/series bible is "a detailed reference document that houses ALL the critical information about your story universe," with five standard sections: character profiles (traits, backstories, relationships, arcs), setting and world rules, timeline/chronology, plot outlines and subplots, and themes/motifs. The payoff is continuity: "Nothing pulls readers out of a story faster than inconsistent details," and for a series it "ensures that details from Book 1 align seamlessly with Books 2, 3, and beyond." For serialized writing in a git repo this maps naturally to a versioned bible file per book, updated in the same commit as each chapter. Sanderson's own practice is the same idea in plain tooling: headings in a Word document for setting, character, and plot.

Source: Atmosphere Press, "Creating a Story Bible for Your Book or Series"; practice corroborated by Brandon Sanderson's 2025 worldbuilding lecture -- <https://atmospherepress.com/creating-a-story-bible/>

## Caveats

(1) GRRM's "Aragorn's tax policy" quote is real (Rolling Stone, 2014) but rollingstone.com was unreachable from this environment (DNS blocked); the quote was verified via Liz Bourke's essay which reproduces it, and that same essay argues the critique misreads medieval state capacity (Tolkien-era kingdoms could not have a "tax policy" in the modern sense). Treat it as a design prompt, not settled doctrine. (2) The "iceberg principle" is Hemingway's prose-omission theory from Death in the Afternoon; applying it to worldbuilding is a later community extension. Hemingway never wrote about fantasy worlds. Sanderson teaches the same idea as the "illusion of depth". (3) Sanderson is sometimes quoted with a "Zeroth Law" ("Always err on the side of what's awesome"); it appears in his talks and the third-law essay's lore, but it was NOT present on any page fetched here, so it is excluded from findings. Sanderson also stresses his laws are guidelines for his style of fiction, not universal rules. (4) The exact Tolkien phrase "the inner consistency of reality" is standard in scholarship, but the Wikipedia page fetched paraphrases rather than quotes it verbatim; the finding uses the sourced paraphrase. (5) Several preferred primary-ish sources 403'd or DNS-failed from this environment (SFWA.org, Mythcreants, MasterClass, Writer's Digest, Reedsy's worldbuilding guide, tolkiengateway); equivalents were substituted. Wrede's questionnaire was fetched from her own site instead of SFWA's mirror. (6) The naming and series-bible sources (Loreteller, Atmosphere Press) are reputable craft/publisher blogs but not author-primary; their rules align with Sanderson's lecture advice fetched directly.
